# Music & lyric craft

Writing bars, co-writing, breaking down tracks, engineering emotion, prompting Suno, and the flow/structure knowledge behind hit songs.

## Suno V5 music prompting
GENRE STACKING: combine 2-3 genres with connectives: "[genre1], [genre2] elements, [mood] energy" (e.g. "trap rock, orchestral elements, cinematic energy"; "dark pop, gothic choir, melancholic but powerful"). MAX 3 genres — more = muddy.

BPM/KEY: use ranges ("80-95 BPM"), not exact ("87 BPM"). Key mood descriptors: "minor key emotional weight", "haunted tonality", "triumphant major key".

VOCAL STYLE KEYWORDS: "melodic trap vocals", "auto-tuned melisma", "aggressive rap delivery", "whispered hook", "belted chorus", "harmonized background vocals", "spoken word verse". Delivery: "emotionally raw", "stoic", "haunted", "battle-ready", "vulnerable whisper".

ARRANGEMENT: bass/drums ("808 bass dominant", "punchy trap kicks", "rolling hi-hats", "stuttered snare"); texture ("orchestral strings beneath the trap", "cinematic brass hits on the 2", "piano melody carrying the hook"); space ("lo-fi warmth", "stadium-sized reverb", "tight room acoustics", "ethereal reverb tail"); dynamics ("builds to a wall of sound in the chorus", "drops to minimal beat in the verse", "explosive final chorus hit").

GOOSEBUMPS TRIGGERS: "unexpected key change in the bridge", "vocal drop to near-silence before the final chorus hit", "orchestral swell rising beneath the 808", "callback to the intro melody in the outro", "choir entering under the final hook".

STRUCTURE TAGS (in the lyrics, each on its own line): [intro], [verse], [pre-chorus], [chorus], [hook], [bridge], [break], [outro].

WHAT KILLS THE TRACK: more than 4 genre tags (muddy); conflicting moods in one prompt ("happy but sad but dark but uplifting"); long prose instead of stacked keywords; too many technical instructions at once.

## Co-writing lyrics (collaborative, never solo)
The artist brings the concept, feeling, and lived experience. You bring structure, wordplay density, rhyme architecture, and taste. Two separate deliverables: (1) LYRICS — the actual bars with section headers; (2) a separate production/style prompt block (BPM, genre tags, vocal style, instrumentation, mood).

PROCESS:
1. LISTEN FIRST — if they share a track, analyze before suggesting. What's working? Strongest bar? Weakest? Emotional core?
2. IDENTIFY THE DIRECTION — what does THIS project want to be (dark, bright, weird, aggressive, tender…)? Just ask "what are we making?" and go — never offer a menu of emotional modes.
3. HOLD THE STANDARD — every bar earns its spot. If it could appear in any generic song, rewrite it.
4. SPECIFICITY OVER GENERALITY — concrete images; the more detailed, the more universal.
5. DOUBLE DUTY — the best bars work on two levels at once (luxury + doom, confidence + vulnerability, literal + metaphor).
6. RHYME ARCHITECTURE — internal rhymes, multisyllabic patterns, rhyme webs — not just end rhymes. Density without sacrificing clarity.
7. FLOW AWARENESS — write to the beat: triplet for hypnotic sections, double-time for climaxes, conversational for storytelling, behind-the-beat for dominance.
8. PUSH BACK ON LAZY BARS — never a yes-man. "You wrote [strongest bar] — hold the verse to that standard."
9. REWRITE, DON'T REPLACE — show your alternative alongside their original; let them choose. Their voice > your suggestion.

Never use cliché emotional language ("neon lights", "whispers in the dark"); never stuff syllables to force a rhyme — if it doesn't flow naturally, find a different rhyme.

## Breaking down a track (real feedback, not compliments)
STRUCTURE:
1. THE VOCAL — delivery, dynamics, emotional range. Is the vocal living in one lane or does it have contrast? Where should it whisper vs push?
2. THE LYRICS — section by section. Quote the strongest bars and why they hit; quote the weakest and what's wrong. Be specific.
3. THE MIX — by the numbers if audio analysis is available (bass weight, stereo width, spectral centroid, LUFS, dynamic range). Translate to feeling.
4. WHAT TO WORK ON — numbered, specific, actionable ("rewrite the first four bars of verse 1 from the corruption imagery in bar 5"), not "make it better".
5. VERDICT — honest summary: best thing about it, what's holding it back, what the best version sounds like.

PRINCIPLES: lead with what works; be specific; compare the artist to THEMSELVES not other artists; translate technical critique to feeling ("0.06 stereo width" means nothing — "sounds like a phone speaker even on good monitors" means everything); never sugarcoat; always end with a path forward.

VOCAL DYNAMICS MAP: intro 40% · verse building 50→65% · chorus 75% (let production carry) · bridge DROP to 30% (nearly whispered — where emotional truth lives) · outro dissolving/fading.

MIX RED FLAGS: bass under 5% = no floor; stereo width under 0.15 = essentially mono, sounds cheap; spectral centroid above 10K = too top-heavy; dynamic range under 6 dB = crushed; peak above -0.3 dB = clipping risk.

## Writing cold bars — punchline architecture
Every cold bar has 3 parts: (1) LEAD-IN — sounds normal, sets expectation; (2) PIVOT — the word/phrase with double meaning; (3) PUNCH — lands the alternate meaning, recontextualizes the lead-in.

PUNCHLINE TYPES: wordplay/double entendre (one word, two meanings); simile punch (the comparison is the surprise); callback punch (set up an image in verse 1, flip it in verse 3); reversal punch (start positive, end dark); stack punch (multiple meanings in rapid succession).

WHAT MAKES IT COLD: delivery matters as much as the words — the pause before the punch; don't explain it, let it land; confidence, no hesitation; it should work on first listen AND reveal new meaning on replay.

CADENCE: slow down right before the punch to give it space; switch flow patterns (steady rhythm then unexpected pause); internal rhyme in the setup, end rhyme on the punch; monotone setup → inflection on the punch word.

## Wordplay & lyrical technique
DOUBLE ENTENDRE LEVELS: single-bar punchline (Wayne); sustained metaphor (Jay-Z — a whole verse inside one framework); mosaic rhymes (Eminem — multi-word phrases sharing vowel patterns); architectural concept (Nas "I Gave You Power" — a song narrated by a gun); album-wide thematic (Kendrick).

RHYME DENSITY: MF DOOM "Vomitspit" ~43% of syllables rhyme (highest measured); Eminem word-bending (orange → door hinge); Big Pun multisyllabic chains; Rakim invented internal rhyming (before him, rap only rhymed at line ends).

When checking bars: layered meaning (second read?), rhyme density (internals/multisyllabics or just end rhymes?), specificity (the specificity paradox — the more detailed the image, the more universally relatable).

## Flow types and their emotional effect
- Triplet flow: groups of 3 per beat. Hypnotic, trance-like (Three 6 Mafia → Migos "Versace").
- Double-time: twice the syllables per bar. Claustrophobic urgency (Eminem "Rap God"). A climactic gear shift, not the whole song.
- Melodic flow: blurs rapping and singing. Opens vulnerability (Drake "Marvin's Room"; Young Thug treats voice as texture).
- Conversational flow: sounds like natural speech, hides complex internals underneath (Biggie "Juicy"). Builds trust.
- Behind-the-beat: syllables land slightly after the pulse (Snoop). Effortless dominance — the rhythm waits.
Best writers switch flows mid-song. When writing vocal direction, name the flow and why: "Verse 1: conversational, building trust. Chorus: melodic, opening up. Verse 2: double-time gear shift. Bridge: behind-the-beat, dominant."

## Song structure intelligence
Dominant hit pattern: verse → pre-chorus → chorus → verse → pre-chorus → chorus → bridge → chorus.
Streaming-era rules: intros under 5s (25% of songs get skipped in the first 5s); hit the hook within 50s (Max Martin); average hit length ~3:17; choruses repeat ~3.8x; post-chorus (secondary earworm) is a modern innovation; sweet spot = enough repetition for familiarity + enough variation to avoid boredom.
Max Martin techniques: syllable mirroring (consecutive lines, identical syllable counts); recycle chorus melodic fragments inside the verse (subconscious pre-hook); "sweet and salt" contrast (loose verse → locked chorus); escalate each chorus; only 3-4 melodic parts, one new element at a time.

## Engineering musical frisson (goosebumps)
Frisson is a measurable response. Stack 2-3 triggers at the SAME moment; one goosebump moment per song beats forcing it every 30 seconds.
- HARMONIC: unexpected chord changes; appoggiatura (a note that clashes then resolves — Adele "Someone Like You"); deceptive cadence; key change up a half step before the final chorus; silence before the drop.
- DYNAMIC: sudden volume shift (quiet verse → explosive chorus); layering instruments one at a time; the strip-down (vocal only for 2-4 bars, then everything back); sub-bass you feel below 60Hz.
- LYRICAL: the callback (reference verse 1 in the last verse with new meaning); the reveal (a line that recontextualizes everything); vulnerability after bravado; specificity ("3am on the kitchen floor" > "I was sad").
- VOCAL: controlled rasp/break on emotional notes; whisper-to-belt; dragging behind the beat; unexpected harmony entering; the falsetto crack.
- FREQUENCY: 2-4kHz presence boost on vocals at emotional peaks; sub-bass 30-60Hz for chest vibration; 10-16kHz shimmer on strings/pads during builds.
- PRODUCTION: reverb swell (dry verse → reverb opens on chorus); side-chain pumping ~120bpm; tape saturation on the final chorus; vinyl crackle for intimacy.

## Making music feel distant and lonely
PRODUCTION: less is more (one instrument + voice); long reverb tails with pre-delay (300-500ms); low-pass everything except the vocal; slightly detuned/flat piano; single sustained bass note; remove the kick (no heartbeat = no life); vinyl crackle/tape hiss; field recordings (rain, distant traffic, room tone).
MELODY: descending lines; minor pentatonic; large interval jumps (reaching); repeat one phrase (stuck in a loop); end phrases unresolved.
LYRICS: second-person past tense ("you used to…"); concrete details of absence ("your side of the bed is cold"); time references ("3am", "the Sunday after"); objects as stand-ins ("your coffee cup is still on the counter").
VOCAL: breathy, close-mic'd; slightly flat on purpose; no ad-libs; long pauses between lines; speak some lines instead of singing.
SUNO: genre tags "lo-fi/ambient/minimal/sparse"; mood "melancholic/introspective/nocturnal/desolate"; instruments "solo piano/acoustic guitar/ambient pads"; avoid "upbeat/energetic/anthem"; keep it short (2:30-3:00), no bridge; add "reverb-heavy, distant vocals, tape warmth".

## Neuroscience of musical emotion
- Dopamine: music triggers the same reward circuits as food/sex/drugs — anticipation ("wanting", caudate) and peak ("liking", nucleus accumbens). Proven pharmacologically.
- Frisson: sudden loudness, unexpected chord changes, new instrument entry, tempo shifts. The brain predicts what's next; broken predictions surge dopamine. ~20% of people get frisson regularly.
- Music-induced crying: prolactin (grief-comfort hormone) releases without real loss → a pleasurable, calming cocktail.
- Heavy sub-bass literally makes listeners feel more powerful (activates social-power patterns).
Use this to explain WHY a choice works: "the beat switch at the bridge triggers a dopamine surge — the brain expected continuation and got disruption"; "heavy 808s in the chorus make the listener feel powerful."

## Producer technique reference
- Timbaland: rhythm-first, beatboxes patterns, stuttering kicks, kick pitched to the song's key, Eastern instrumentation fusion.
- Pharrell/Neptunes: minimalism + groove, dry punchy drums with space, jazzy major-7th chords, unconventional bar structures (3-bar phrases crashing into 4).
- Metro Boomin: 808 as melody (tuned to the key with pitch slides), space is the philosophy, haunting minimal piano over sparse precise drums.
- Kanye: sampling as collage — chipmunk soul, 808s minimalism, industrial. Samples invoke memory/nostalgia/irony at once.
Reference by name when relevant: "that beat has Metro energy — the 808 is doing the melody work"; "your arrangement is too full — Pharrell would strip half of this and let the groove breathe."
