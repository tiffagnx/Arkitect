# EQ recipes by instrument (starting points, finish by ear)

## How do I EQ a lead vocal

Put EQ-6 or DeMartin EQ first in the chain. Start with a high-pass around 80-100 Hz to clear rumble and stage/handling noise (push to 120 Hz on a thin/high voice, but stop before it sounds boxy). A static HPF won't fully kill plosives — those need mic technique/a pop filter, or a steeper low-cut automated only on the popped word. Most "muddy/boxy" vocal lives at 200-400 Hz — sweep a narrow cut there and pull 2-4 dB where it sounds congested. For clarity and consonant cut-through, a gentle 1-2 dB shelf or bell around 3-5 kHz; for "air" and breath, a high shelf around 10-12 kHz, +1-3 dB. If 5-8 kHz gets harsh or sibilant, that's a job for De-Ess (next in the chain), not a big EQ cut. These are starting points — every voice and mic is different, so sweep, listen in the full mix, and trust your ears. The Vocal Doctor button builds this whole chain for you as a baseline.

## How do I EQ backing vocals and harmonies

Backing vocals should sit behind the lead, not fight it. High-pass higher than the lead, around 120-200 Hz, since they don't need weight. Then carve a complementary dip: wherever the lead vocal's presence lives (often 3-5 kHz), pull backing vocals down 2-4 dB there so the lead stays on top — this is "carving a pocket." Roll off some air above 10 kHz if the stack gets fizzy, and cut 200-500 Hz more aggressively than on the lead to keep the stack from building mud. Stacked doubles add up fast: route them all to an Aux Input bus and EQ the bus once instead of fighting each track. Pan and level do as much as EQ here — finish by ear in context.

## How do I EQ a kick drum

Two zones define a kick: the low thump and the click/beater attack. The body/thump sits around 50-100 Hz — boost a couple dB there for weight, or high-pass below 30-40 Hz to clear sub rumble that just eats headroom. The classic mud/boxy zone is 300-500 Hz; a 2-5 dB cut there cleans it up fast. The beater click that lets a kick punch through a loud mix lives around 2-5 kHz — bell up 2-4 dB to taste. The single most important kick move is to make room for it against the bass/808: pick one to own the deep sub and carve the other. Use DeMartin EQ's live spectrum to see exactly where the two collide. Starting points only — genre dictates whether you want boom or click forward.

## How do I EQ a snare drum

A snare needs body, crack, and snare-wire sizzle. High-pass around 80-100 Hz to drop kick bleed and rumble. The fundamental "fatness" is roughly 150-250 Hz — boost a little for a thicker snare, cut if it's tubby. The boxy/honky problem zone is 400-800 Hz; a narrow 2-4 dB cut there opens it up. The crack/attack that cuts through is around 3-5 kHz, and the wire snap/brightness is up at 7-10 kHz — boost either to taste. If you want more sustain and ring, parallel compression does more than EQ. These are starting points; a deep tom-sounding snare and a tight pop snare want very different curves, so listen in the mix.

## How do I EQ hi-hats and cymbals

Hats and cymbals are almost all high-frequency content, so the biggest win is a high-pass — sweep it up to 300-600 Hz (sometimes higher) to remove all the low junk and bleed they don't need. The harsh, spitty zone is typically 2-5 kHz; if cymbals feel piercing or fatiguing, a narrow 2-4 dB cut there tames them. For sparkle and shimmer, a high shelf above 10 kHz, +1-3 dB. Overheads are different from a spot hat mic — overheads carry the whole kit's air, so high-pass them lower and be gentle. Watch for sibilance-style harshness colliding with a bright vocal; if both are fizzy, carve one. Starting points — bright trap hats and jazzy ride cymbals call for opposite treatments.

## How do I EQ an 808 or bass

The golden rule is decide who owns the sub: the 808/bass or the kick — they can't both. High-pass very low, around 25-35 Hz, to clear inaudible rumble that wastes headroom and triggers limiters. The fundamental power sits around 50-100 Hz; the note definition and "where you hear the pitch on small speakers" lives around 80-250 Hz. Mud/boom is 200-400 Hz — cut a couple dB if it's woofy. For an 808 to translate on phones and laptops, add midrange grit with the Saturator (harmonics let small speakers imply the missing sub) rather than just boosting bass. To stop kick/808 fighting, carve a notch in one (usually the 808) at the other's fundamental so each owns its band; or, for a pumping ducked sound, draw volume automation on the 808 that dips a few dB on every kick hit. Starting points — a clean R&B bass and a distorted trap 808 want different curves.

## How do I EQ an electric guitar

Electric guitar lives mostly in the mids, so don't waste effort on extremes. High-pass around 80-120 Hz — there's nothing useful below the low E for most amp tones, and it just adds mud. The boxy/mud zone is 200-500 Hz; cut a few dB if it's congested, especially when stacked with bass and other guitars. The "honk" or nasal zone is 800 Hz-1.5 kHz — narrow cut if it's harsh. Presence and bite that help it cut through sit around 2-4 kHz. Above 6-8 kHz you mostly get amp fizz/hiss, so a gentle high shelf cut often cleans it up. When two guitars clash, EQ them to occupy different ranges (one brighter, one darker) instead of boosting both. Starting points — finish against the vocal and drums.

## How do I EQ an acoustic guitar

Acoustic guitar can get boomy from the body resonance and harsh from the pick. High-pass around 80-100 Hz to lose rumble and handling noise. The boomy/woofy problem zone is 100-300 Hz — a 2-4 dB cut there is the most common acoustic fix, especially in a dense mix where it clashes with vocals and bass. The boxy zone is 300-500 Hz. For string detail and "sparkle," boost gently around 5-10 kHz; for air, a high shelf above 10 kHz. If the pick is too clicky/scratchy, look around 2-4 kHz. In a full mix you can high-pass an acoustic surprisingly high (up to 150-200 Hz) so it sits as a texture under the vocal. Solo recordings keep more low end — these are starting points.

## How do I EQ piano and keys

Piano spans the full spectrum, so the move is usually to subtract for space, not boost. In a busy mix, high-pass around 60-100 Hz to keep the low keys from clashing with bass and kick. The mud zone is 200-400 Hz — cut a couple dB if it sounds thick or cluttered behind vocals. Carve a dip around 1-4 kHz where the vocal's presence lives so the voice stays in front. For brightness and hammer detail, a gentle lift around 5-8 kHz; for air, a high shelf above 10 kHz. A solo piano ballad wants almost no EQ — keep the full range. A pad-style Rhodes or electric piano can be high-passed and mud-cut much more aggressively. Starting points — context decides.

## How do I EQ synths and pads

Synths are made to fill space, so subtractive EQ to make room for other elements is usually the priority. High-pass pads and leads around 100-200 Hz unless the synth is the bass — let the bass and kick own the low end. Cut the mud zone 200-500 Hz to keep a wall of synths from turning to soup, and carve a dip wherever the vocal sits (often 2-4 kHz) so the synth supports rather than masks it. Bright leads and supersaws can get harsh around 3-6 kHz — narrow cut to tame. Use the Saturator to add harmonic character instead of boosting highs that introduce harshness. Pads love a wide stereo and a low-mid carve; a lead wants focused mids. Starting points — a sub-bass synth and an airy pad need opposite treatment.

## How do I EQ strings and orchestral

Real and sampled strings need warmth without mud and air without screech. High-pass around 40-80 Hz for high strings (violins/violas) and lower for cellos/basses — don't cut the body out of low strings. The mud/thick zone is 200-400 Hz; trim a couple dB if a section sounds congested behind a vocal. The harsh, scratchy bow noise sits around 2-5 kHz — a gentle narrow cut smooths an overly aggressive sample. For lush air and silk, a high shelf above 8-10 kHz, +1-2 dB. A solo violin wants its bite and rosin left in; a background string pad can be carved hard to sit under everything. Reverb (DeMartin Reverb or a shared Aux send) does more for "lush" than EQ. Starting points — finish by ear in the arrangement.

## Why does my mix sound muddy and what should I cut

Mud is almost always a buildup of low-mids around 200-500 Hz across many tracks at once — no single instrument is the culprit, they stack. Instead of one big cut, make small 2-3 dB dips in that range on the worst offenders: rhythm guitars, piano, pads, thick synths, and the low side of vocals. High-passing tracks that don't need low end (vocals, guitars, hats, overheads) is the highest-leverage mud fix there is — anything that isn't bass or kick rarely needs much below 80-100 Hz. Use DeMartin EQ's live spectrum to spot where energy piles up, and the Auto-mix to apply bounded cleanup moves as a starting point. Solo nothing while judging mud — it only reveals itself in the full mix. These are starting points; over-cutting lows makes a mix thin, so go gently and trust your ears.
