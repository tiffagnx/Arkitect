# Bus, group processing and glue compression

## What is a bus and how do I set one up

A bus is a shared destination you route several tracks into so you can process and ride them as one. In Studio, make an Aux Input track (this creates a bus), then on each source track add a send and point it to that bus, OR set the source tracks' output to the bus. The Aux becomes your "return" where shared FX live. Common groups: drum bus, vocal bus, instrument/synth bus, and an FX bus for reverb/delay returns. The point is one fader and one chain controlling many tracks at once, so they move and breathe together.

## Why does grouping make my mix sound more cohesive

Cohesion comes from many sources sharing the same processing fingerprint. When a drum bus runs one compressor, all the drums duck and recover on the same envelope, so they feel like one kit instead of separate samples. The same EQ tilt, saturation harmonics, and a touch of the same reverb make tracks sound recorded in the same room. In Studio, route like instruments to one Aux and put the shared EQ-6/Compressor/Saturator on that Aux. Glue is mostly small, consistent moves applied across a group rather than big individual fixes.

## How do I set up bus glue compression (settings)

Add a Compressor to the bus (Aux insert) and aim for gentle, musical control, not squashing. Start: ratio 1.5:1 to 2:1, attack 10 to 30 ms (slow enough to let transients punch through), release 100 to 300 ms or "auto," threshold set so you see only 1 to 2 dB of gain reduction on the loudest hits. Match output to input so it's not just louder. The test: bypass and the mix should feel slightly looser; engage and it should feel more "together." If it pumps unpleasantly, lengthen the release or raise the threshold.

## How do I get the SSL-style glue on the drum bus

On the drum-bus Aux Compressor try ratio 2:1 or 4:1, attack 10 to 30 ms, release set to "auto" or timed to the groove (about a quarter-to-eighth note), threshold for 2 to 4 dB of gain reduction on hits. A slower attack lets the kick and snare transients poke through while the compressor pulls up the room and cymbals between hits, which is the "pumping in time" excitement people chase. Set release by ear so the meter recovers just before the next beat. For more aggressive glue, push gain reduction higher, but parallel it (below) to keep punch.

## How do I use parallel (New York) compression on a bus

Parallel compression blends a heavily compressed copy under the dry signal so you add density and sustain without killing transients. In Studio, send the drums (or vocals) to a second Aux, put a Compressor on it set aggressive (ratio 4:1 to 8:1, fast attack 1 to 5 ms, release 100 to 250 ms or auto, 6 to 12 dB gain reduction), then blend that Aux fader up underneath the main group until it thickens. Use pre-fader sends if you want the parallel level independent of the source faders. Mute the dry to hear how smashed the parallel is, then tuck it back in until it just supports.

## How much bus saturation should I add for glue

Light saturation on a bus adds harmonics that fuse tracks and create perceived loudness and warmth without raising peaks much. Add a Saturator to the bus and start very subtle: just enough drive that bypassing it makes the group sound slightly flatter or thinner. Tape/console-style saturation also softens harsh transients. On a mix or master bus keep it gentle (a hair of drive); on a drum or synth bus you can push harder for attitude. Trust your ears and A/B at matched loudness, because saturation almost always sounds "better" just by being louder.

## How do I gain stage into a bus so it doesn't clip

Set levels so the bus input sits in a healthy range and the bus compressor reacts the way you dialed it. If source faders are slamming the bus, the compressor over-compresses and the Aux can clip. Pull the group's overall level with the bus/sub-mix fader rather than re-tweaking every track. Aim for peaks well below 0 dBFS on the bus (a few dB of headroom) so master-bus and limiter stages have room to work. Studio's Record-level coach and the dB I/O readouts on the strip help you confirm you're not pinning the meters. Re-check glue compressor gain reduction after any big level change.

## What's the difference between a send and routing the output to a bus

Routing a track's output to a bus moves 100 percent of that track through the bus (used for grouped processing like a drum bus). A send taps a copy at an adjustable level while the track still reaches its own output (used for shared effects like reverb/delay, where you want the dry signal plus a controllable amount of wet). In Studio you have 10 sends per track (banks A to E and F to J), pre or post fader. Use output-to-bus for sub-mixes and glue; use sends to an FX Aux for parallel and effects returns. Post-fader sends follow the fader; pre-fader sends stay put when you ride the track.

## How do I use sub-mix faders to control my mix

A sub-mix (group) fader is the bus fader that rides a whole section at once. Route all drums to a drum Aux, all vocals to a vocal Aux, all music to an instrument Aux, then balance the song with three or four faders instead of forty. This makes broad moves fast and reversible: pull the whole band down under a vocal, ride the chorus drums up, automate a section lift on one fader. Set the internal balance of each group first, then mix the groups against each other. It also gives you obvious places to hang glue compression and bus EQ.

## Where should I EQ — the track or the bus

Fix problems on individual tracks (a resonant snare ring, a muddy single guitar) and shape the whole group's tone on the bus. A small broad tilt on the bus, like a gentle high shelf around 10 kHz for air or a slight dip near 200 to 400 Hz to clear mud, affects every track consistently and helps them sit as one. Use DeMartin EQ (8-band, live spectrum) on the Aux for these moves and keep them wide and small (1 to 3 dB). Reach for surgical narrow cuts on the source track, not the bus. Always A/B at matched loudness.

## What order should I process on a bus

A common, safe order on a group Aux: subtractive EQ to clean problems, then glue Compressor, then tone EQ, then Saturator last for harmonic glue and warmth. Putting broad tone EQ after the compressor lets you shape the already-glued sound, and saturation last fuses everything. There's no law here, though: putting saturation before the compressor changes how the comp reacts, and some engineers compress first to tame peaks before driving. Try both and keep what sounds more cohesive. Whatever the chain, match bypassed vs engaged loudness so you judge the effect, not the volume.

## How do I set up a shared reverb or delay bus

Make an Aux, put TIFF VERB / DeMartin Reverb (or Tape Delay / the slapback plugin) on it, and feed it from multiple tracks via sends so they share one space. Set the reverb to 100 percent wet on the return and control the amount with each track's send level, which keeps the dry signal clean and lets vocals, snare, and synths sound like they're in the same room. Use post-fader sends so the effect level follows the fader as you ride a part. This is core glue: a common space ties a mix together. The FX-Throw bar can also push tempo-synced throws to a whole selection or a single word.
