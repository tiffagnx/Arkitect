# Diagnose & fix a bad mix — symptom-to-fix guide

## Why does my mix sound muddy or cloudy
Mud lives in the low-mids, roughly 200-500 Hz, where too many tracks pile up — bass, kick body, guitars, synth pads, and the low end of vocals all stack there. Open DeMartin EQ on the worst offenders (usually the non-lead pad/guitar/keys) and pull a wide cut of 2-4 dB around 250-350 Hz; sweep a narrow boost first to find the exact ugly spot, then cut there. High-pass anything that isn't bass or kick: roll off below 80-120 Hz so they stop fighting for low-mid space. Mud is almost always too many sources at once, not one bad track — so cut on several tracks a little rather than one track a lot. Check on speakers and headphones, since rooms exaggerate this range.

## Why is my low end boomy or one-note
Boomy means a resonant buildup, usually a room mode or an over-tuned bass/kick in the 60-120 Hz region that booms on certain notes. Solo the bass and kick together, then sweep a narrow DeMartin EQ boost from 50-150 Hz to find the note that jumps out, and cut it 3-5 dB with a moderate Q. If the bass is "one-note" (every note the same loudness), put a Compressor on it with a fast attack (5-10 ms), medium release (~100 ms), and 3:1-4:1 ratio to even the dynamics, then add a touch of Saturator to give upper harmonics that read as pitch on small speakers. Make sure kick and bass don't occupy the exact same Hz: park the kick fundamental around 60-80 Hz and the bass body just above, or vice versa, and high-pass everything else off the sub.

## Why does my mix sound harsh or sibilant
Harshness sits in the upper-mids and lows of the treble, about 2-5 kHz (aggression) and 6-9 kHz (sibilance/"sss"). For vocal sibilance, reach for the De-Ess plugin first, set the threshold so it only ducks on the harsh "s" sounds, targeting 6-8 kHz — don't over-do it or the singer lisps. For general 2-4 kHz harshness across guitars, synths, or a bright vocal, use DeMartin EQ with a narrow cut of 2-4 dB; sweep to find the exact stab. If a Saturator or limiter is driven too hard it manufactures harshness, so back those off before EQ-ing. The Vocal Doctor's "Smooth" and "De-Ess" macros are the fast path for a single harsh vocal.

## Why does my mix sound thin or weak
Thin means missing low-mid and low-end body, usually because you high-passed too aggressively or the arrangement has a hole around 100-300 Hz. On the lead vocal or main instrument, ease the high-pass down (try 70-90 Hz instead of 150 Hz) and add a gentle DeMartin EQ boost of 1.5-3 dB around 150-250 Hz for chest/warmth. A little Saturator adds harmonics that make a thin source feel fuller without raising the fader. Check that you don't have many tracks all high-passed at the same frequency, which leaves the whole mix hollow. The Vocal Doctor "Warm" macro is the one-mover for a thin vocal.

## Why does my mix sound boxy or honky
Boxy is a hollow, cardboard quality around 300-600 Hz; honky/nasal is the "talking through a tube" zone around 800 Hz-1 kHz (sometimes up to 1.5 kHz). The fix is the same: sweep a narrow DeMartin EQ boost through 300 Hz-1 kHz on the offending track, find the spot that sounds most like a box or a duck, and cut it 2-4 dB with a medium-narrow Q. Guitars, snares, and close-mic'd vocals are common culprits. Don't over-scoop the whole region or the source goes thin and distant — make a surgical cut, not a valley. If several tracks share the same boxiness, a small cut on each beats a big cut on one.

## Why does my mix sound dull or dark
Dull means a lack of air and presence up top. First check you didn't roll off the highs with an over-aggressive low-pass or too much De-Ess. On the lead vocal and overheads/cymbals, add a gentle DeMartin EQ high-shelf of 1.5-3 dB starting around 8-10 kHz for "air," and a small presence lift around 3-5 kHz to bring detail forward. Saturator can restore sparkle on a dark source by generating high harmonics, useful when EQ alone just amplifies hiss. Be careful adding air to everything at once — it stacks into harshness fast; lift the lead and a couple of supporting elements, not the whole mix. The Vocal Doctor "Bright" macro does this safely on a vocal.

## Why does my mix sound small or narrow
Small usually means a mono-ish, dry, centered mix with no depth or width. Create an Aux Input (which makes a bus), put DeMartin Reverb / TIFF VERB on it, and send several tracks to it so they share one space — shared reverb glues a mix and adds depth far better than a separate reverb per track. Pan supporting elements (doubles, guitars, keys) out left and right while keeping lead vocal, bass, kick, and snare centered. Add front-to-back depth by using shorter/drier reverb on what should feel close and longer/wetter on what should feel far. A slapback delay (80-120 ms) on a vocal thickens and adds depth without an obvious echo — note that a mono slapback adds size, not stereo width; true width comes from panned doubles or a stereo reverb/delay return.

## Why does my mix sound too busy or cluttered
Too busy is an arrangement problem more than an EQ problem — too many sounds competing for the same space and moment. Use the mute test: mute each track in turn and ask "did the mix get worse?" If muting a part doesn't hurt, leave it muted or use it sparingly. Carve frequency lanes so each element owns a range (bass low, vocal mid, cymbals top). Use automation or volume rides so parts step back when the vocal or lead enters. Sometimes the fix is editing, not mixing: thin a doubled part, shorten a busy reverb tail, or chop out fills that crowd the vocal.

## Why is my mix loud but still sounds bad
Loud-but-bad means you pushed the Maximizer/limiter for level instead of fixing balance and tone underneath. Pull the limiter back, mix the song so it sounds great at a quiet, comfortable level first, then add loudness last. Clipping and over-limiting cause pumping, distortion, and a squashed, lifeless sound — watch true-peak and keep it at or below -1 dBTP. Don't chase a number blindly, but streaming services normalize playback to roughly -14 LUFS integrated, so pushing far past that just gets turned back down while keeping the squash. Loudness comes mostly from good balance, controlled dynamics (Compressor on the loud sources), and clean low end, not from slamming the master. Reference a commercial track at the SAME volume to judge honestly, since louder always seems "better" for a few seconds.

## Why is my vocal buried in the mix
A buried vocal is usually masked by instruments sharing its frequency range or sitting too quiet/dynamic. First raise the fader — many "buried" vocals just need 1-3 dB. Then carve space: on the busiest competing tracks (guitars, synths, pads) make a small DeMartin EQ cut of 2-3 dB around 2-4 kHz where vocal presence lives. Tame the vocal's own dynamics with a Compressor (3:1-4:1, attack ~10 ms, release ~80-120 ms) so quiet words don't disappear. A touch of presence boost (3-5 kHz) and air (10 kHz) on the vocal helps it cut. Vocal Doctor builds this whole chain in one click — start there, then ride the fader by hand for the lines that still drop.

## Why does my mix have no punch
Punch is transients and contrast — the snap of the kick and snare and the dynamic gap around them. Don't over-compress: a too-fast/too-heavy Compressor kills the transient that IS the punch. On kick/snare, set a slower attack (15-30 ms) so the initial hit passes through before the Compressor grabs, with a fast-ish release to recover. Make sure the low-mid mud (200-400 Hz) is cleaned up so the punch isn't smeared. A little Saturator on the drum bus adds harmonic bite that reads as punch on small speakers. Punch also comes from arrangement space — if everything plays constantly, nothing hits.

## Why does my mix have no low end (or too much)
No low end usually means over-high-passing the bass/kick or a phase cancellation between them. Check the bass isn't high-passed above ~40 Hz and that kick and bass aren't fighting — solo them together and EQ so they interlock (carve a dip in one where the other's fundamental lives). Too MUCH low end is the opposite: high-pass every non-bass track at 80-120 Hz, and use DeMartin EQ to trim the sub region on busy tracks. Control bass dynamics with a Compressor so the level is steady, then judge on headphones or a known reference, since most speakers and rooms lie about the bottom octave. Open DeMartin EQ's live spectrum analyzer on the master, not your gut, to see how much energy is actually sitting below ~120 Hz.

## Why does my mix sound fatiguing or tiring to listen to
Ear fatigue comes from sustained energy in the 2-5 kHz range, over-compression that removes all dynamic relief, and too much loudness. Sweep for a buildup around 3-4 kHz across the brightest sources and make small DeMartin EQ cuts; resist boosting highs everywhere. Loosen compression so the mix still breathes — if everything is flat-line loud, ears tire in seconds. Pull the master Maximizer back so peaks have somewhere to go and you're not living against the ceiling. Mix at a quieter monitoring level for longer sessions; fatigue is partly just listening too loud. Take breaks — fresh ears catch harshness you've gone deaf to.

## How does frequency masking work and how do I carve space
Masking is when two sounds in the same frequency range fight, and the louder/closer one hides the other — that's why a great-sounding solo'd track can vanish in the full mix. The fix is to give each element its own lane: decide who owns each range (kick and bass share the lows, vocal owns presence, cymbals own the air) and EQ accordingly. The classic move is complementary EQ — boost a track where it should shine and cut the SAME frequency on the track that's masking it. Example: boost vocal 3 kHz a touch, dip the rhythm guitar 3 kHz by 2-3 dB. Panning also un-masks — move two clashing parts to opposite sides and they stop fighting. Don't try to make every track full-range; let things be small so the mix can be big.

## What is the mute test and solo-in-context
The mute test: with the full mix playing, mute one track at a time and ask "did the mix get worse, or did it get cleaner?" If muting a part doesn't clearly hurt, that part may be clutter — cut it, lower it, or use it only in key moments. Solo-in-context is the opposite habit: judge a track WHILE the rest of the mix plays, not in isolation, because a track only has to sound good in the song, not alone. A thin, weird-sounding solo'd guitar often sits perfectly in the mix; a lush solo'd one often muds it up. Use solo only to find a problem (a click, a resonance), then make the actual EQ/level decision unsolo'd. Mixing is about relationships, so make decisions in context.

## How do I know when a mix is done (less is more)
A mix is done when more moves make it worse, not better — chasing tiny tweaks past that point usually undoes good work. Less-is-more means reaching for a cut before a boost, an arrangement edit before a plugin, and a fader move before an EQ; the cleanest fix is often muting something. Take frequent breaks and A/B against a commercial reference at matched loudness to reset your ears. Sleep on it: a mix that sounds finished tonight reveals problems with fresh ears tomorrow. Watch for plugin-stacking — if a track has six inserts doing 1 dB each, simplify. When the balance feels right at low volume, translates on phone/earbuds/speakers, and you stop wanting to change things, it's done.
