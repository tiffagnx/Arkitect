# Creative Reverb Tricks

## Reverse reverb — the swell that builds INTO a word
Reverse reverb is that whoosh of reverb that crescendos right up to the front of a word, then the dry word lands. The trick: the reverb plays forward in time on the reversed audio, so when you flip it back the tail becomes a build. Exact steps in Studio: 1) select the clip (one word or a vocal phrase) and hit Reverse. 2) Add TIFF VERB / DeMartin Reverb to that track and dial a long tail — decay 2.5-4 s, predelay 0, 100% wet feels right for the effect, then use Print VERB to bake the reverb into the clip. 3) Reverse the clip again so the word plays normally. Now the reverb tail you printed plays backwards and swells into the word. Trim the dry attack tight and crossfade the swell into the line. Great on the first word of a verse, a chorus entry, or a one-shot vocal stab.

## How do I make reverse reverb in this DAW step by step
There's no one-click reverse-reverb button yet, so you build it from three clip ops. Duplicate the source clip first (Ctrl+D) so you keep the dry original. On the copy: Reverse, then put DeMartin Reverb on the track (long decay ~3 s, wet high), then Print VERB to commit the wet tail onto the clip, then Reverse again. Drag the resulting swell so its peak lines up exactly with where the dry word starts. Use an Equal-Power fade-in on the swell so it rises smoothly instead of clicking on. If it sounds too obvious, shorten the printed tail or lower the wet level before printing. Keep the dry word on top so the consonant still bites.

## Gated reverb — the big 80s snare sound
Gated reverb is a huge bright reverb that gets chopped off abruptly instead of decaying naturally — the Phil Collins / "In the Air Tonight" snare. The reverb is loud and dense, then a noise gate slams it shut after a short window so there's no lingering tail. To build it in Studio: send the snare (or whole drum bus) to an Aux Input with DeMartin Reverb on it — pick a bright plate-ish setting, decay 1.5-2.5 s, predelay 0-10 ms. Then put a Gate after the reverb on that aux. Set the gate to a fast attack, hold ~80-150 ms, and a fast release so it cuts dead. Tune the hold by ear to the tempo; longer = more drama. Roll a little off below 200 Hz on the reverb so it doesn't get boomy. It's an effect, not realism — commit hard.

## Shimmer reverb — the angelic pitched-up tail
Shimmer reverb feeds the reverb tail back through a pitch shift (usually +12 semitones, one octave up) so the wash keeps blooming brighter and higher — that ambient, Eno/worship-pad halo. Studio doesn't have a built-in shimmer module, so fake it: send the source to an Aux with a long reverb (decay 4-8 s). On a second aux, take the reverb return, Tune that printed tail up +12 semitones with DeMartin Tune, and blend it back under the original. The deeper way is to print the reverb tail, pitch the printed clip up an octave, add reverb again, and stack two or three of those layers. Keep the shimmer quiet under the dry source — it's a glow, not the main event. High-pass it around 300-500 Hz so it stays airy.

## Freeze or infinite reverb tail — holding a wash forever
An infinite/frozen reverb tail sustains a chord or note into a pad that never decays — great for transitions, intros, and ambient beds. Studio has no one-click freeze, so print it: solo the moment you want to hold, send it to an Aux with DeMartin Reverb set to maximum decay (8 s+), and Print VERB / Bounce that aux to a clip. Then loop or time-stretch that printed wash to fill the gap, and fade it in/out with Equal-Power fades so the seam is invisible. For a swelling pad, layer two prints slightly detuned with DeMartin Tune (±5-10 cents). High-pass around 200-300 Hz and tuck the level under the dry mix so it supports rather than smears.

## Reverb throw on the last word into a gap
A reverb throw is when one word — usually the last word of a line — gets drenched in reverb that blooms out into the empty space before the next line. It adds drama without washing out the whole vocal. Easiest way in Studio: use the FX-Throw bar, which does tempo-synced reverb/delay throws on a whole selection OR a single word — select just that word and throw it. Manual way: automate the reverb send up only for that word (banks A-J), or duplicate the word to a parallel "throw" track that's 100% wet. Time the tail to the gap — a 1/4 or 1/2-note predelay/decay synced to the BPM keeps it musical. Throws live in the holes between phrases; if the next line steps on the tail, shorten it.

## Should I print or commit my reverb
Printing (committing) reverb bakes the wet effect permanently onto the clip instead of leaving it as a live plugin. Pros: it's CPU-free, it locks the sound so it can't drift if you change plugins later, and it's required for tricks like reverse reverb and frozen tails where you need the wet signal as actual audio. Cons: it's harder to undo — so always Duplicate (Ctrl+D) the dry clip first and keep it muted nearby. In Studio, select the clip and use Print VERB to bake the track's reverb in, or Bounce to Disk (bus/stem) for a whole reverb return. Rule of thumb: keep reverb live while you're mixing decisions, print it once a creative effect is final.

## Predelay, decay, and EQ — the three knobs that fix muddy reverb
Most reverb problems come down to three controls. Predelay is the gap before the tail starts (10-40 ms) — more predelay keeps the dry vocal clear and pushes the reverb behind it, so the words stay intelligible. Decay/RT is how long the tail lasts (0.8-1.5 s for tight pop vocals, 2-4 s for ballads and ambient) — match it loosely to the tempo so tails clear before the next phrase. EQ the reverb itself: high-pass the return around 200-400 Hz to kill boom, and dip a little 2-5 kHz or low-pass above 8-10 kHz if it gets harsh or sibilant. In Studio, put DeMartin EQ or EQ-6 on the reverb's Aux return, after the reverb. Add a De-Ess before the reverb send so esses don't splash.

## Reverb send vs insert — the pro routing
Putting reverb on a send/aux instead of as an insert lets many tracks share one reverb, keeps the dry signal fully present, and gives you one place to EQ and automate the wet. Pattern in Studio: make an Aux Input (it creates a bus), put DeMartin Reverb on it set to ~100% wet, then send each vocal/drum/instrument to that bus (10 sends per track, banks A-E / F-J). Leave sends post-fader (the default) so the reverb level follows the track fader; switch a send to pre-fader only for special effects where you want the wet to stay put even when you pull the dry down. One short "vocal plate" aux and one long "ambient hall" aux covers most songs. Automate send amounts to push reverb up on held notes and pull it back in busy sections. Insert reverb only when you want it welded to a single sound.

## Predelay tricks and tempo-synced reverb
Syncing reverb to the song's tempo makes tails feel intentional instead of random. Set predelay to a note value: at 120 BPM a 1/16 note is ~125 ms, an 1/8 is ~250 ms — a 1/16 predelay lets the vocal speak before the tail blooms in rhythm. Use Studio's Bars|Beats timebase to see the grid, and the FX-Throw bar for tempo-synced throws. For rhythmic ambience, feed reverb from a tempo-synced slapback or Tape Delay / BPM Delay first, then into the reverb aux — the repeats give the tail a pulse. Match decay so the tail mostly clears by the next downbeat; long tails over fast tempos turn to mush. This is taste, not law — sometimes an unsynced wash is exactly the vibe.
