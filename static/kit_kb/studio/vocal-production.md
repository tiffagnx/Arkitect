# Pro Vocal Production, End to End

## What order should my vocal chain go in

The reliable order is: clean up the recording, then tune, then subtractive EQ, then de-ess, then compression (often two light stages), then saturation, then a presence EQ boost, then reverb/delay on sends, and finally automation to ride every word. Each step makes the next easier: tuning before EQ means you're shaping the right pitches, cleaning before compression means you're not pulling up noise, and de-essing before saturation means you're not exciting harsh sibilance. The one-click version is the Vocal Doctor 🩺 button on the vocal's Mix strip — it builds EQ-6 → De-Ess → Compressor → Saturator → slapback plus a reverb send in roughly this order. Build it by hand when you want full control; use the Doctor to get 90% there fast, then fine-tune each insert. Order isn't religion — some engineers tune after a little compression, or saturate before EQ — but this sequence is the safe default.

## How do I clean up a vocal before mixing

Clean first so every later move works on the voice, not the noise. Drop a Cleanup (denoise) insert to pull down hiss, hum, and room buzz, and use Strip Silence on the clip to gate out the dead space between phrases where breaths and noise live. Edit obvious mouth clicks and loud breaths by hand: select the spot and use clip-gain or cut/fade, or shape them with Fades (a short Standard fade on a breath tames it without killing realism). Don't strip every breath — a few keep the vocal human; just lower the distracting ones. High-pass later in the EQ stage will also remove subsonic rumble. Clean stage is about removing what shouldn't be there, not tone-shaping yet.

## Should I tune natural or use the hard auto-tune effect

That's a creative choice, and the difference is mostly retune speed. For natural, transparent tuning, open Tune (DeMartin Tune, the Melodyne-style editor), correct only the notes that are clearly off, and pull them gently toward pitch rather than dead-center — leaving small natural variation and vibrato keeps it sounding human. For the hard auto-tune effect (the T-Pain/modern-rap snap), you want pitch fully quantized to the scale with the fastest possible retune so notes glide instantly between steps — that artifact IS the sound. In DeMartin Tune, move notes onto the grid and minimize the transition time for the effect; loosen it for natural. Set the correct key/scale first so the editor snaps to the right notes. Many pop vocals sit in between — fairly corrected but not robotic. Tune before EQ and compression so you're shaping in-tune material.

## What is retune speed and how fast should I set it

Retune speed is how quickly the tuner drags a sung note to the target pitch. Slow retune (think hundreds of milliseconds — loosely 100-400 ms+) preserves the natural slides, scoops, and vibrato a singer does on the way into a note, so corrections sound invisible. Fast/instant retune (near zero, only a few ms) snaps the pitch immediately to the nearest scale note, which kills the slide and creates the hard auto-tune "snap" between words. For a natural lead, favor a slower setting and only tighten on notes that wander. For the effect, set it as fast as it goes and make sure the scale is locked so it only snaps to in-key notes. In DeMartin Tune you control this per-note by how far and how abruptly you move each note to the grid. Tune by ear against the instrumental, not just to the visual grid.

## How do I do vocal doubles, stacks, and ad-libs

Doubles thicken the lead by layering separate performances. Best is to record the same line two-plus times and pan them apart (one ~30% left, one ~30% right) under the center lead — real takes sound wider than copies. If you only have one take, Duplicate (Ctrl+D) the clip, nudge the copy 10-30 ms, and pan it to one side — panning is what keeps the slight delay from comb-filtering against the original; a centered copy just smears the lead. A real second take always beats a copy. Stacks (many layers) get bussed: make an Aux Input (creates a bus), send all the doubles to it, and treat them as one group with shared EQ and compression so they glue and sit behind the lead. Ad-libs are the call-outs and runs panned and thrown wide with effects — give them their own track, push them lower in level, and lean on delay/reverb sends so they sparkle around the lead without crowding it. Keep doubles slightly quieter and a touch duller than the lead so the lead stays the focus.

## How do I make vocal harmonies sit right

Harmonies support the lead, so they should be present but never compete. Record real harmony parts when you can; to fake them, Duplicate the lead clip onto a new track and use Tune (DeMartin Tune) to move the notes to the harmony interval (a third or fifth above/below in key) — set the scale first so shifts stay musical. Bus all harmonies to an Aux Input and process them as a group: high-pass higher than the lead (~150-250 Hz) to clear room for it, roll off some presence so they're less bright than the lead, and compress them a little harder so they're steady and don't poke out. Pan harmony pairs out to the sides for width. Keep them several dB under the lead and add a bit more reverb so they tuck behind it. If a harmony fights the lead for attention, it's too loud or too bright.

## How do I EQ a vocal — subtractive then presence

Two distinct EQ jobs sit on opposite ends of the chain. Early, subtractive: with DeMartin EQ (8-band, live spectrum) or EQ-6, high-pass around 80-100 Hz to remove rumble, then sweep a narrow cut to find boxy/muddy buildup (often 200-500 Hz) and harsh/honky resonances (1-4 kHz) and pull them down a few dB — use the live spectrum to spot peaks. This cleans the tone before compression so the compressor isn't reacting to mud. Later, presence/air (after compression and saturation): a gentle wide boost around 3-5 kHz brings the words forward and improves intelligibility, and a high shelf up around 10-12 kHz adds "air" and sheen. Boost broadly and modestly (1-3 dB); cut narrowly and as deep as needed. Whether to boost or cut is dictated by the sound, not by any rule.

## How do I stop my vocal from being harsh or sibilant

Harsh "sss" and "ttt" sounds come from sibilance, and a De-Ess insert is the right tool — it compresses only the sibilant frequency band. Place it after subtractive EQ and before heavy compression/saturation (those stages exaggerate sibilance). Set the De-Ess to listen around 5-8 kHz for most voices (higher for bright/female voices, lower for darker ones) and pull only 3-6 dB on the loudest esses — enough to soften them without lisping the singer. The Vocal Doctor 🩺 inserts a De-Ess automatically and gives you a Smooth/De-Ess macro clamped to a safe range. If a couple of words are still spitting after de-essing, automate the fader or clip-gain down just on those syllables rather than de-essing harder across the whole take. General harshness (not just esses) is better fixed with a narrow EQ cut in the 2-5 kHz region.

## How do I add saturation and warmth to a vocal

Saturation adds harmonics that make a vocal feel richer, louder, and more "finished" without raising the peak level much, and it helps the voice cut through a dense mix. Use a Saturator insert after compression so you're coloring an already-controlled signal. Start subtle — just enough drive that you hear it thicken, then back off until you're not sure it's on; that's usually the sweet spot. Light tape-style saturation adds warmth and gentle compression; harder drive adds grit and presence for aggressive styles. De-ess before saturating, because saturation can sharpen sibilance. The Vocal Doctor's chain includes a Saturator with a Warm macro on a safe range. A little across the whole vocal glues it; for an effect, push a parallel saturated copy on an Aux and blend it under the dry vocal.

## How do I use reverb and delay on vocals without washing it out

Put time-based effects on sends, not inserts, so the dry vocal stays clear and you control the wet amount independently. Make an Aux Input (creates a bus) with TIFF VERB / DeMartin Reverb on it, then use the track's sends (banks A-E / F-J) to feed it — a short plate or room (decay ~1-1.5 s) sits a vocal in a space without obvious tails. Add a separate delay send (Tape Delay or the slapback plugin); a slapback (~80-120 ms, one repeat) adds depth, while a tempo-synced 1/8 or 1/4 delay adds rhythmic space — the FX-Throw bar can tempo-sync a throw on the whole selection or a single word. Keep effects subtle on the main lead and push them on ad-libs/hooks. High-pass the reverb/delay return (~300 Hz) and roll off its highs so the wash doesn't muddy or hiss. To dial-in reverb amount fast, the Vocal Doctor adds a reverb send with a Space macro.

## How do I ride the vocal so every word sits

Riding (volume automation) is the secret that separates pro vocals — even after compression, individual words and phrases drift, so you automate the fader (or clip-gain) up on words that get buried and down on words that jump out. Compression levels the fast peaks; automation handles the slow, musical balance the compressor shouldn't chew on. Listen against the full mix and write fader moves phrase by phrase: pull down a too-loud line, push up a mumbled word, lift the last word of a phrase that trails off. Doing some of this with clip-gain before the compressor means the compressor works less and sounds more natural. This is tedious and worth it — it's why every lyric stays intelligible in a loud mix. Auto-mix can apply bounded leveling moves to get you started, but final word-by-word rides are done by ear.
