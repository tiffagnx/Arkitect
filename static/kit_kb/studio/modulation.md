# Modulation Effects — Chorus, Flanger, Phaser, Tremolo, Vibrato, Rotary

## What is a modulation effect and which one do I want
Modulation effects move some part of the sound over time using an LFO (a slow wave). What gets moved decides the flavor: chorus and flanger move tiny delay times to create movement and width, phaser moves notches in the frequency response for a sweepy "whoosh," tremolo moves volume up and down, vibrato moves pitch, and rotary spins sound around like a Leslie speaker. Pick by the job: want a thin part to sound fuller and wider, reach for chorus; want a dramatic jet/whoosh sweep, flanger or phaser; want rhythmic pumping in volume, tremolo. The DeMartin DAW has no dedicated modulation plugin yet, so most of these are built by automating or hand-tooling the delay/EQ/volume tools described below.

## How do I thicken and widen a thin vocal or synth with chorus
Chorus copies the signal, delays the copy a few milliseconds, and slowly wobbles that delay so the copy drifts in and out of tune with the original. That doubling is what makes a thin part sound lush and wide. Starting points: delay 15–35 ms, LFO rate 0.3–1.5 Hz, depth/modulation small (a few ms of wobble), and mix the wet copy at 30–50%. Keep the rate slow and the depth modest or it starts sounding seasick. In the DAW, the honest approximation is doubling: duplicate the clip (Ctrl+D), nudge the copy 15–35 ms, and pan the two apart for width. Note this is a static doubler/Haas widener, not real chorus — without an LFO there's no movement, so it won't shimmer. A true LFO chorus needs a manual workaround that the DAW can't really do cleanly today, so it is on the gaps list.

## How do I get that jet plane flanger whoosh
Flanger is chorus's intense cousin: very short delay (about 1–10 ms) swept by an LFO, fed back on itself so it creates a moving comb-filter that sounds like a jet taking off. The feedback is what gives it the metallic, resonant peak. Starting points: delay 1–5 ms, LFO rate 0.1–0.5 Hz for slow sweeps, feedback 40–70% for the strong "jet," mix 50%. More feedback equals more drama and ring; pull it back if it whistles. In DeMartin Studio, build it with a Tape Delay set to an extremely short time with feedback turned up, automated by ear, or print a swept version and blend it. A one-knob flanger does not exist yet, so it is a gap.

## What is the difference between flanger and phaser
They sound similar but work differently. A flanger uses a short modulated delay, which makes evenly-spaced notches across the spectrum (harmonic comb filtering) — it sounds metallic and jet-like. A phaser uses all-pass filters to create a few notches that are NOT evenly spaced — it sounds smoother, more "vowel-y" and swirly, less metallic. Rule of thumb: flanger is aggressive and resonant, phaser is gentle and watery. For guitars and electric pianos a phaser usually sits more politely in a mix; for a big obvious sweep effect, flanger wins. Neither is built one-click in the DAW today; phaser especially is hard to fake and is on the gaps list.

## How do I add sweepy movement with a phaser
Phaser stacks all-pass filters whose phase shift moves with an LFO, creating notches that sweep up and down — classic on Rhodes, clean guitar, and pads to keep a static part interesting. Starting points: 4–6 stages, LFO rate 0.1–0.5 Hz, depth moderate, feedback/resonance low to medium, mix 40–60%. Slower rate equals a longer, more hypnotic sweep; more stages and feedback equal a more vocal, pronounced character. The DeMartin DAW has no phaser plugin, so to fake the vibe you can automate a narrow notch on the DeMartin EQ (sweep one band's frequency by hand) — it is a crude approximation, not a true phaser. Add this to gaps.

## How do I make a part pulse rhythmically with tremolo
Tremolo modulates VOLUME up and down with an LFO — it does not touch pitch. It gives that pulsing surf-guitar or helicopter throb and can sync to the beat. Starting points: rate 4–8 Hz for a fast shimmer, or sync to 1/8 or 1/16 notes for rhythmic pumping; depth 30–60% for subtle, up to 100% for full on/off chops. Sine shape is smooth; square shape gives a hard gated chop. In DeMartin Studio you make tremolo by drawing volume automation on the track as a repeating wave, or by using Chop to 1/16 plus fades on alternating slices for a hard gated effect. A tempo-locked tremolo knob is not built in yet — gaps list.

## How do I add pitch vibrato (different from tremolo)
Vibrato modulates PITCH up and down with an LFO — think the wobble a singer or violinist adds at the end of a note. It is chorus without the dry signal (chorus = dry plus pitch-modulated wet; vibrato = only the modulated wet). Starting points: rate 4–7 Hz, depth small (a few cents to a quarter-tone) for natural, wider for a seasick effect. Too much depth sounds out of tune fast, so use a light hand on vocals. In the DAW, the cleanest path is DeMartin Tune (the Melodyne-style pitch editor) — draw or add pitch-drift/vibrato on a held note directly. For instruments, automating a fine pitch wobble is not one-click, so a dedicated vibrato is on the gaps list.

## What is a rotary or Leslie effect and when do I use it
A rotary (Leslie) speaker physically spins a horn and a bass drum, so you get a swirling blend of pitch shift (Doppler), volume tremolo, and stereo movement all at once — iconic on organ, and great on guitar, Rhodes, and even vocals for vintage swirl. The magic detail is the speed switch: slow (chorale, ~0.8 Hz) for a gentle shimmer and fast (tremolo, ~6–7 Hz) for an excited whirl, with a short ramp as it spins up or down between them. There is no rotary plugin in DeMartin Studio yet — you can suggest the vibe by combining slow chorus-style duplication, gentle tremolo automation, and stereo panning, but it will not nail the Doppler. This is firmly on the gaps list.

## Why does my stereo chorus or widener vanish in mono
This is the big trap. Most stereo chorus, flangers, and "wideners" create width by putting out-of-phase or time-shifted copies in the left and right channels. When that gets summed to mono — club PA, phone speaker, Bluetooth, many streaming downmixes — those copies partially cancel and the part gets thin, hollow, or disappears. Always check a mono sum before committing. In DeMartin Studio, pan the two duplicated clips toward center and listen for the part shrinking; if it does, reduce the width or keep some of the energy mono-safe by leaving a centered dry copy. Bass and lead vocals especially should stay strong in mono.

## What is the safe way to widen without killing mono compatibility
Width built from short delays and phase tricks is fragile in mono; width built from genuinely different content is robust. Safer moves: pan two real takes (or a duplicated clip nudged a tiny bit) hard left and right so the difference is timing/performance, not pure phase; keep low frequencies (below ~150 Hz) centered and mono since wide bass is what cancels worst; and apply heavy widening to background/ear-candy parts rather than the lead vocal or kick. Use modest chorus mix (30–40%) instead of cranking it. Always A/B in mono in the DAW before bouncing. When in doubt, narrower and solid beats wide and disappearing.

## Should modulation go before or after delay and reverb, wet or dry
Order is taste, but the common starting chain is: EQ and dynamics first, then modulation (chorus/flanger/phaser/tremolo), then time effects (delay, reverb) last so the space wraps around the modulated tone. Modulating BEFORE reverb keeps the reverb tail clean; modulating the reverb's output gives a swirling, dreamy tail. For subtlety run modulation on an insert at a low mix; for an obvious special effect run it higher or on a send (make an Aux in DeMartin Studio, send the track to it, stack the effect there) so the dry track stays solid. Once you love it, lock it in by bouncing the track (Bounce to Disk, or stem) and re-importing — there's no one-click "freeze/print insert" for these hand-built chains, so this commit step is the manual route.

## What starting settings should I use for each modulation effect
Quick reference starting points, then tune by ear. Chorus: delay 15–35 ms, rate 0.3–1.5 Hz, light depth, mix 30–50%. Flanger: delay 1–5 ms, rate 0.1–0.5 Hz, feedback 40–70%, mix ~50%. Phaser: 4–6 stages, rate 0.1–0.5 Hz, low-to-medium feedback, mix 40–60%. Tremolo: rate 4–8 Hz or sync to 1/8–1/16, depth 30–100%. Vibrato: rate 4–7 Hz, depth a few cents to a quarter-tone. Rotary: slow ~0.8 Hz / fast ~6–7 Hz with a ramp between. Slower rate equals smoother and more hypnotic; deeper/more-feedback equals more dramatic and risky. Always check mono after dialing in any stereo modulation.
