# Metering & referencing — meters, LUFS, and trusting your ears

## What do LUFS, RMS, peak, and true-peak actually mean

These are four different ways to read loudness, and they answer different questions. Peak (sample-peak) is the single highest sample value — it tells you about clipping risk, not perceived loudness. True-peak estimates the analog waveform reconstructed between samples and rides a hair higher than sample-peak; it's what matters for the master ceiling (aim for -1.0 dBTP). RMS is a short-window average of energy — a rough density read. LUFS (Loudness Units relative to Full Scale) is the modern standard: it weights frequencies the way ears hear and integrates over time to match perceived loudness. Use peak/true-peak to avoid distortion, and LUFS to judge how loud the song actually feels. Read all of these off the master meters and the Maximizer/limiter at the end of the chain.

## What LUFS should my master be for streaming

Integrated LUFS is the whole-song average loudness, and a common target is around -14 LUFS integrated with true-peak at or under -1.0 dBTP — that lines up with where many streaming platforms normalize. But this is not a sacred number: hand a quiet -16 LUFS ballad and a loud -8 LUFS hip-hop master to the same platform and it turns the loud one down, so chasing a louder number buys you nothing on streaming and can cost you dynamics. Set the loudness that serves the SONG, check the integrated LUFS over the full track, and keep true-peak under -1.0 dBTP on the Maximizer so it survives MP3/AAC encoding. Genre and taste decide the rest — dense, aggressive music sits louder and accepts more limiting; dynamic music breathes lower. Louder is not better; appropriate is better.

## Why reference a commercial track and how do I gain-match it

A reference is a finished, professionally mixed song in your genre that you A/B against your mix to catch problems your ears have gone numb to. The single most important rule: gain-match first. Louder always sounds "better" for a few seconds, so if the reference is louder than your mix you'll wrongly think the reference wins on everything. Pull the louder one down (drop the reference's track fader) until both sound equally loud by ear or read the same short-term LUFS, THEN compare tone, low end, width, and vocal level. Drop the reference onto its own track in Studio, use mute/solo to flip between it and your mix, and listen for what yours is missing — usually it's balance, not gear. Pick 2-3 references, not ten.

## What is phase correlation and how do I check it in Studio

Correlation describes how related your left and right channels are, on a scale from +1 to -1. +1 means the channels are identical (effectively mono); 0 means wide, decorrelated stereo; negative values mean the channels are partly out of phase, which causes weak, hollow sound and partial cancellation when summed to mono. A healthy full mix sits mostly positive, dipping toward 0 on wide moments; the bass and kick especially should stay centered and near +1 for mono compatibility. Studio doesn't have a dedicated correlation meter, so the mono check IS your phase test here: collapse to mono and listen for elements that thin out, weaken, or vanish — that's negative correlation made audible. Fix it by narrowing the offending wide element (stereo reverb, a doubled/widened synth) or flipping a polarity-reversed mic.

## How do I do a mono check and why does it matter

Summing the left and right channels to mono cancels anything out of phase between them — so the mono check is the fastest way to expose phase problems and over-widening. Sounds disappearing, the lead vocal getting weaker, or the low end thinning out in mono all point to phase issues you'd never hear in stereo. It matters because tons of real listening is mono or near-mono: phone speakers, single Bluetooth speakers, club PA subs, laptop speakers, far-field in a noisy room. Studio has no one-click mono button, so the practical check is to Bounce to Disk and play the file through a single phone or Bluetooth speaker — a real mono sum — early and often, not just at the end. If something vanishes, narrow the wide element, check mic polarity, or pull stereo reverb/widening back until the part survives. A mix that holds up in mono almost always sounds bigger in stereo too.

## How do I read a spectrum analyzer without chasing a flat line

A spectrum analyzer shows energy across frequency, low (20 Hz) on the left to high (20 kHz) on the right, and it's a map, not a verdict — your ears decide, the analyzer confirms. Use it to SEE what you suspect: a bump around 200-400 Hz when things sound muddy, a spike at 2-5 kHz when it's harsh, a hole in the low mids, or sub energy below 30 Hz you can't even hear on your speakers. Do NOT EQ your mix into a flat or textbook curve — real records have tilt and bumps that vary wildly by genre. The DeMartin EQ has a live spectrum behind its draggable bands, so you can watch a frequency region while you move a band by ear. Reference tracks are gold here: overlay or A/B your spectrum against a commercial song to spot whether your low end or top end is genuinely off.

## Why should I mix at low volume and on multiple systems

Loud playback flatters everything — bass feels huge, the top sparkles, problems hide — because of how our hearing responds to level (the ear's frequency balance changes with volume). Mixing mostly at LOW volume (quiet enough to talk over) forces the important elements to balance themselves: if the vocal and the groove read clearly when it's quiet, the balance is solid. Then check key decisions louder briefly, but don't live there. Just as important, audition the mix on multiple systems — your monitors, cheap earbuds, a phone speaker, the car, a laptop — because each reveals different flaws. The car exposes low-mid buildup and bass tuning; the phone exposes whether the vocal and kick survive a tiny mono speaker; earbuds expose harshness and stereo. If it translates everywhere, it's done. Bounce to Disk to get a real file onto your phone and car.

## Why do my ears stop hearing problems and how do I fix it

Ear fatigue is real: after 45-90 minutes your hearing dulls, you stop noticing harshness, and you creep the volume and the vocal up to compensate — the classic sign is each pass getting louder and brighter. The fixes are dead simple. Take a 10-15 minute break every hour, leave the room entirely, and let your ears reset in silence. Mix at lower volumes to slow fatigue down. Make your big tonal and balance decisions early in a session when your ears are fresh, and save tedious editing for when they're tired. Sleep on the final mix and judge it cold the next morning — problems that hid behind fatigue jump out instantly. If you catch yourself riding the master fader up repeatedly, stop; that's fatigue, not the mix needing to be louder.

## Should I trust the meters or my ears

Ears decide, meters confirm — never the other way around. Meters are honest about things ears lie about: gain-matching for fair A/B, true-peak ceiling for clean encoding, integrated LUFS for delivery, phase correlation, and seeing sub energy you can't hear on small speakers. But no meter knows whether the vocal sits right, whether the groove pockets, or whether the song moves you — only listening at low volume on real systems tells you that. The trap is "mixing with your eyes": EQ'ing to a pretty curve or pushing LUFS to a number while the mix sounds worse. When the meter and your ear disagree, investigate, but the ear wins on tone and feel. Use meters to catch what fatigue and loudness hide, then close your eyes and decide.

## What's a fast pro referencing workflow inside Studio

Build a referencing setup once and reuse it. Import 2-3 commercial reference songs onto their own tracks, gain-match each to your mix by pulling the reference's track fader down until short-term LUFS reads the same, then mute/solo to flip instantly between yours and theirs. A/B at LOW volume, and listen for one thing at a time — low end, then vocal level, then top-end brightness, then width. Watch the DeMartin EQ spectrum on both to confirm tonal differences you hear. Then bounce your mix (Bounce to Disk, WAV + MP3) and the same section of the reference, play both off your phone and in the car, and do your mono/phase check on a single phone or Bluetooth speaker. Note the 2-3 biggest gaps, fix only those, and re-reference fresh the next day rather than tweaking endlessly while fatigued.
