# Compression explained — punch, sustain, and the right character

## What does a compressor actually do
A compressor turns down the loudest parts of a signal once they cross a level you set, then you bring everything back up. The result is a smaller gap between the quiet and loud moments, so the track sits more evenly and feels louder and steadier without you riding the fader. Add a Compressor on a track strip with + insert. The four controls that matter are threshold (where it starts working), ratio (how hard), attack (how fast it grabs), and release (how fast it lets go). Makeup gain at the end restores the level you lost. Everything else is taste built on those four.

## How do I set threshold and ratio
Threshold is the level where gain reduction begins; lower it and more of the signal gets compressed. Ratio is how much it clamps once past threshold: 2:1 is gentle, 4:1 is a workhorse, 8:1 and up is aggressive (limiting territory). Start at 4:1, then lower the threshold until the GR meter shows reduction on the loudest words or hits, then back off. A good general vocal or bass starting point is 3:1 to 4:1 with the threshold set for a few dB of reduction. For glue on a bus, go gentler: 1.5:1 to 2:1. There is no "correct" ratio — set it for the amount of control the part needs and judge by ear.

## How much gain reduction should I aim for
Watch the gain-reduction (GR) meter, not the threshold number — the number means nothing without knowing the input level. For a lead vocal, 3 to 6 dB of GR on the loud syllables is a solid everyday target; peaks can touch 8 to 10 dB if the take is wild. For bus or mix glue, keep it tiny: 1 to 3 dB, just enough that the needle breathes with the music. Drums can take more, 4 to 8 dB for a punchy smash. If the meter is pinned and never returns to zero, your threshold is too low or release too slow. Less reduction with the right attack and release almost always beats more reduction.

## How does attack shape punch and transients
Attack is how fast the compressor clamps down after the signal crosses threshold. A SLOW attack (20 to 50 ms) lets the initial transient — the pick, the snare crack, the "p" of a word — punch through before compression grabs the body, so the track sounds punchier and more aggressive. A FAST attack (0.1 to 5 ms) catches that transient too, taming the spike and making the sound smoother, rounder, more controlled, but it can dull the snap. For drum punch, start slow around 20 to 30 ms. For taming a spiky vocal or bass, go faster, 5 to 15 ms. Adjust by ear: if it sounds flat and lifeless, lengthen the attack to let transients through.

## How does release shape sustain, pump, and breathing
Release is how fast the compressor stops working after the signal drops back below threshold. A FAST release (40 to 100 ms) brings the level back up quickly, which raises sustain and tail, adds energy and aggression, but if it is too fast on the wrong source you get audible "breathing" or distortion. A SLOW release (200 to 500 ms+) is smoother and more transparent but can sound squashed or leave the track ducked. "Pumping" is the compressor recovering in time with the beat — sometimes a fault, sometimes a deliberate effect on drum buses. A great trick: set release so the GR needle returns to zero just as the next beat hits, so the comp moves with the groove. The Compressor's Release runs 20 to 800 ms — if you are unsure, start around 150 ms and adjust by ear.

## What is knee, soft knee vs hard knee
Knee controls how abruptly compression kicks in around the threshold. A HARD knee clamps suddenly the instant the signal crosses — precise and obvious, good for limiting, drums, and surgical control. A SOFT knee eases compression in gradually over a few dB before and after threshold, so the transition is smoother and far less audible — better for vocals, bass, and anything you want to sound natural. If you want gentle, invisible leveling, choose a soft knee and a moderate ratio. If you need to stop transients hard, choose a hard knee. When in doubt, soft knee is the more forgiving choice for melodic material.

## What does makeup gain do and how to set it
Compression lowers your loud peaks, so the whole track gets quieter — makeup gain (often "output" or "gain") adds that level back. Set it so the compressed signal is roughly as loud as the bypassed signal, then A/B with the Compressor's bypass to compare fairly. This matters because louder almost always sounds "better" at first, so if your compressed version is louder you will fool yourself into thinking the compression helped when it was just the volume. Match levels, then decide if the dynamics control is actually an improvement. The Compressor's Makeup runs -12 to +24 dB — set it by hand to match bypass; there is no auto-gain, and that is fine, matching by ear takes seconds.

## Which compressor character — VCA, FET, Opto, Vari-mu
Real hardware comes in flavors and each has a vibe. Studio's Compressor has no topology selector, but you get close to each one by how you set attack, ratio, and knee. VCA (clean, fast, precise) is the all-rounder for buses, drums, and tight control — fast attack capable, transparent. FET (very fast, aggressive, colored) is the classic loud-vocal and drum-room sound — punchy, attitude, grabs transients hard; reach for it when you want energy and bite. Opto (smooth, program-dependent, slower) is gentle and musical on vocals and bass, with a release that softens as it works — great for natural leveling. Vari-mu (tube, slow, glue-y) thickens and rounds whole mixes and buses. Choose by the job: control = VCA, attitude = FET, smooth vocals = Opto, glue = Vari-mu. It is a tone choice, not a quality ranking.

## How do I compress a vocal — the easy way and the deep way
Easy way: hit the 🩺 Vocal Doctor button on the vocal's Mix strip. It builds EQ-6 → De-Ess → Compressor → Saturator → slapback plus a reverb send, sets safe baselines, then gives you six clamped macro sliders (Bright, Warm, Smooth, De-Ess, Space, Throw) you cannot push into ugly territory. The deep way: insert a Compressor manually, set 3:1 to 4:1, medium attack (5 to 15 ms) so words keep their consonants, fast-ish release (60 to 120 ms) so the tail stays up, and dial the threshold for 3 to 6 dB GR on the loud lines. For very dynamic vocals, stack two light compressors (3 dB each) instead of one heavy one — it sounds more natural. De-Ess before the compressor so it does not pump on sibilance.

## Should I compress on the track, the bus, or both
Compress on the individual track to control that one part's dynamics — a vocal, a bass, a kick. Compress on a BUS (group) to glue several parts into one cohesive unit — make an Aux Input (it creates a bus), send your drums or backing vocals to it, and put one gentle Compressor on the Aux. Bus glue wants a low ratio (around 2:1), slow-ish attack so transients survive, and just 1 to 3 dB of GR. Both at once is normal and powerful: light control on each track, light glue on the bus. Just keep each stage gentle — many small moves beat one violent one, and the reductions add up fast.

## What is parallel compression and how do I do it here
Parallel ("New York") compression blends a heavily crushed copy under your clean track, so you get thickness and sustain while keeping the original's punch and transients. It is how you make drums and vocals sound huge without losing life. Easiest path: the Compressor has a Mix knob (0 = all dry, 1 = all compressed) — smash hard with fast attack and a high ratio, then pull Mix down to about 0.3 to 0.5 so the crushed copy sits under the dry signal, no routing needed. For more control (separate EQ on the squashed path, several sources at once): make an Aux Input (creates a bus), send the track to it PRE-fader (so the blend stays put when you ride the dry fader), and smash a Compressor on the Aux — fast attack, 8:1, 10 dB+ GR. Bring the Aux fader up under the dry track until it adds weight. One caution: if the copy makes the sound thinner or hollow instead of bigger, that's phase cancellation between the paths — nudge the Aux level or skip it on that source.

## Why does my compressed track sound worse, not better
Usually one of four things. One: you are comparing at unmatched volume — set makeup gain to match bypass, then A/B honestly. Two: too much GR — if the needle is buried past 10 dB constantly, raise the threshold or lower the ratio. Three: wrong attack — a too-fast attack killed your transients and made it flat (lengthen it), or too-slow let everything through (shorten it). Four: wrong release — too fast is pumping and distortion (slow it down), too slow is a permanently ducked, lifeless track (speed it up). Bypass often, trust the GR meter over the numbers, and remember subtle is usually the goal — compression is seasoning, not the whole meal.
