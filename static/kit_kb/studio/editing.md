# Editing fundamentals — comping, timing, fades, and clean edits

## How do I comp the best take from multiple takes
Comping means building one perfect performance from several takes. Record your takes onto separate tracks (or duplicate the line), then audition each and Chop to 1/16 (or use the Smart Tool trim) to slice at phrase or word boundaries. Keep the best slice from each take and Mute (Ctrl+M) or delete the losers, then drag the keepers onto one comp track. Edit on phrase boundaries, not mid-word, so the breaths and consonants stay intact. Once the comp reads as one take, Consolidate it into a single clip so it can't drift. Always crossfade every join (see fades) or you'll hear clicks where two takes meet.

## How do I fix the timing of a word or note that's late or early
Use Slip mode so you can nudge freely, then isolate the offending word: Chop to 1/16 around it (or trim its edges with the Smart Tool) so it's its own clip. Drag it earlier or later until it sits with the beat or the rest of the performance. Move by feel against the kick/snare or the other vocals, not by eye. Push the new edge to a transient or a silent gap so the cut is invisible, and add a short crossfade (5-15 ms) into the neighboring clip. For drums, cut right before the transient (the attack spike) so you keep the punch.

## When should I use Slip mode vs Grid mode for editing
Slip mode lets clips move to any position freely — use it for natural timing fixes, comping, and nudging a word by a hair so it grooves. Grid mode snaps edits and moves to the timeline grid (set the division in the Bars|Beats timebase) — use it for chopping loops, lining clips to the bar, stutter edits, and anything that must land exactly on the beat. A common workflow is Grid to rough-place a clip on the downbeat, then Slip to fine-tune by a few milliseconds so it feels human. Switch between them with the Slip/Grid edit-mode control; pick by the job, not by habit.

## How do I make crossfades that hide my edits
A crossfade overlaps the tail of one clip with the head of the next so the seam is inaudible. Select the two adjacent clips and apply Fades (pick the equal-power crossfade shape); for many joins at once use the Batch Fades dialog (Edit, Fades, Create). Length is the key: 3-10 ms for tight rhythmic edits and drum hits, 10-30 ms for vocals and sustained notes, 50 ms+ for ambient or pad material. Too long smears the transient; too short still clicks. If you still hear a bump, move the edit to a zero-crossing or a quieter spot first, then fade. Every comp join and every clip butt-join should get at least a tiny fade.

## What fade shape should I use — Standard, Equal-Power, or S-Curve
The DAW offers three fade shapes and each suits a different join. Equal-Power keeps constant loudness across a crossfade of two DIFFERENT but related sounds (two takes of the same vocal, looped material) — it prevents the dip in the middle, so it's the default crossfade for comping. Standard (linear) is fine for simple fade-ins/outs and quick rhythmic cuts. S-Curve eases in and out gently — great for smooth fade-outs at the end of a phrase or a track, and for de-emphasizing a hard attack. When two clips are phase-coherent (the same recording cut), a linear/standard fade can actually sound cleaner than equal-power. Try both by ear.

## How do I crossfade a whole bunch of edits at once
Use the Batch Fades dialog (Edit menu, Fades, Create) when you have many edits to clean up — a comped vocal, a chopped drum loop, a stutter section. Select the whole region containing all the edit points and open Batch Fades; it shows curve previews and lets you set the shape (Standard / Equal-Power / S-Curve), the fade length in milliseconds, and pick an equal-power crossfade for every join at once. Start around 5-10 ms for vocals, 3-5 ms for drums, then adjust. There are presets (1-5) for common settings. This turns ten minutes of dragging fades by hand into one click.

## What is Heal Separation and when do I use it
Heal Separation rejoins two adjacent clips that came from the same original recording back into one seamless clip — as if you never cut them. Use it when you chopped a clip, decided you didn't want the edit, and the two halves haven't moved relative to each other. It only works when the clips are from the same source and still time-aligned; if you've slid one of them, the underlying audio no longer lines up and the heal won't be clean. It's the fast undo for an unwanted split. After comping, if a join sounds worse than the original take, heal it and re-cut elsewhere.

## What does Consolidate do and why should I use it
Consolidate renders a selection of clips, edits, fades, and silences into one new continuous audio clip. Use it after comping or heavy editing so your carefully assembled performance can't drift, and so the track is lighter and easier to move, copy, or hand off. It bakes in the crossfades you made, so check that every fade sounds right BEFORE you consolidate — it's destructive to the edit structure (keep the pre-consolidate version if unsure). It's also the clean way to print a tidy clip before sending a track to another room or bouncing. Pair it with Strip Silence first if you want gaps removed.

## How do I chop audio to the grid and trim clip edges cleanly
Chop to 1/16 slices the selected clip on every sixteenth-note boundary in the Bars|Beats grid — instant for making stutter edits, gating a pad rhythmically, or prepping a loop for rearrangement. For freer cuts, use the Smart Tool: its trim function drags a clip edge in or out, and its select/grab functions let you grab and move the result. Trim-to-cursor cuts a clip at the playhead. Always land an edge on a transient or in silence, never in the middle of a sustained note, and add a 2-5 ms fade on any exposed edge so it doesn't click.

## How do I quantize audio to tighten it to the grid
Quantizing audio means moving each hit so its transient lands on the grid. The reliable manual method: switch to Grid mode, Chop to 1/16 so each hit is its own clip, then drag each slice so its attack snaps to the nearest grid line, and crossfade the gaps with Batch Fades. For drums this tightens a sloppy take; don't quantize 100% or it sounds robotic — leave some hits slightly off for feel. For pitched/vocal timing, the DeMartin Tune editor (Melodyne-style) lets you move notes in time as well as pitch, which is gentler than slicing. One-shot transient-detect audio quantize isn't one-click here yet, so the chop-and-drag method is the way.

## How do I stop my edits from clicking or popping
Clicks happen when you cut where the waveform isn't at zero — the sudden jump in level is the pop. Two fixes. First, make your edit at a zero-crossing (where the waveform crosses the center line) so there's no level jump. Second, and more reliable, put a tiny fade on every exposed clip edge — even 2-5 ms kills the click. For any join between two clips, use a crossfade instead of a butt-join. The Batch Fades dialog can fade every edge in a selection at once. If a consolidated clip still ticks at its boundaries, re-fade the head and tail before bouncing.

## How do I clean up clip edges and silence between phrases
Trim dead air and noise from the heads and tails of clips so only the wanted audio plays. Use Strip Silence to auto-remove gaps below a threshold (great for tightening a vocal comp or a DI'd guitar), then check each new clip edge. Trim-to-cursor or the Smart Tool trim tightens an edge right up to where the word or note starts. Leave a little breath/room before a vocal phrase — don't clip the consonant or the inhale that makes it feel natural. Add a 3-10 ms fade-in on each cleaned edge and a slightly longer fade-out on tails so silence arrives smoothly. For hiss inside the audio (not the gaps), use the Cleanup denoise plugin rather than cutting.
