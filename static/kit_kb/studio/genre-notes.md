# Genre-Specific Mixing Notes

## How do I mix hip-hop and trap — starting point

The vocal sits on top and the low end is the song. Start the lead vocal loud and present: Vocal Doctor 🩺 builds EQ-6 → De-Ess → Compressor → Saturator → slapback, then push the fader until the vocal sits clearly over the beat (often 3-6 dB louder than you'd expect from other genres). Use a Compressor doing 4-8 dB to keep every word steady and upfront. EQ a touch of air at 10-12 kHz and a small dip around 300-500 Hz to clear mud. The 808 and kick are the foundation, so get their relationship right (see the 808-vs-kick section). These are conventions of the style, not rules — a moody, buried-vocal aesthetic is also valid if it serves the song.

## How do I balance the 808 and kick in trap

The 808 (sustained sub-bass note) and the kick fight for the same low space, and the fix is making room for both. Easiest: keep the kick short and punchy (it lives mostly 50-100 Hz plus click at 2-4 kHz) and let the 808 own the deep sub (30-60 Hz). On the kick's EQ-6 or DeMartin EQ, carve a narrow dip where the 808's fundamental sits so the kick punches through without masking the sub. The pro move is sidechaining: route the kick to duck the 808 a few dB on each hit so the kick's transient pokes through, then the 808 swells back. The DAW has no one-click sidechain compressor yet — emulate it by automating the 808 clip-gain or fader down ~3-6 dB right on each kick, or carve the kick a clean lane with EQ. Check on a sub or headphones, because laptop speakers hide all of this.

## How do I get a sidechain pump in EDM

That breathing "pump" is the bass and pads ducking on every kick, which makes the kick punch and the track feel like it's pulsing. With a real sidechain compressor you'd key the bass/pad bus off the kick at ratio 4:1+, fast attack, release timed to let it recover just before the next kick (about 1/4 to 1/8 note). This DAW doesn't have a sidechain compressor one-click, so do it with automation: draw the bass/pad fader or clip-gain down 4-8 dB on each kick and ramp it back up before the next beat — copy that shape across the section. Match the release feel to tempo: faster recovery on busy 1/16 patterns, slower for a deep house surge. Done right the kick cuts through a wall of synths without you having to turn it up.

## How do I mix an EDM drop so it hits hard

A drop hits because of contrast, not just volume — the build before it should be thinner and the drop should slam wide and full. Keep the build narrower and filtered (high-pass the synths, pull energy out of the lows) so when the drop lands the full spectrum and the sub crash in by comparison. Make the lead synths wide (layer slightly detuned/panned parts, or duplicate and pan a copy left/right) while keeping the kick and sub mono and dead-center for power. Glue the drop with bus compression on an Aux Input (1-3 dB) and let the Maximizer on the master push loudness. Use a tempo-synced reverb/delay throw from the FX-Throw bar on the last build note to sling energy into the drop. Wide synths, mono low end, big contrast — that's the formula.

## How loud and wide should an EDM master be

EDM is mastered loud and is one of the few genres where pushing hard is genre-appropriate. Many club/streaming EDM masters land around -7 to -9 LUFS integrated, hotter than pop — but streaming platforms normalize to roughly -14 LUFS, so going louder mostly buys density and impact, not playback level. Use the Maximizer/limiter as the last master insert and set true-peak to -1.0 dBTP to survive MP3/AAC encoding (Bounce to Disk exports WAV + MP3). Push loudness only until transients start to dull or the kick loses punch — then back off. Keep the sub and kick mono for translation and reserve the width for the synths and effects up top. Loud is a stylistic choice here, not a quality measure — a clean -9 beats a crushed -6.

## How do I mix a pop song — vocal-forward and polished

Pop is built around the lead vocal: it should be the loudest, clearest, most polished element. Run Vocal Doctor 🩺 for the EQ-6 → De-Ess → Compressor → Saturator → slapback chain, then tune it with Tune (DeMartin Tune) — modern pop vocals are pitch-corrected, sometimes obviously, sometimes subtly, your call by taste. Compress firmly (often serial: two Compressors each doing 2-4 dB) so the vocal stays glued and upfront at every level. Add air at 10-12 kHz, a presence lift around 3-5 kHz, and de-ess the sibilance the brightness exposes. Keep everything else clean and supportive: tight low end, no clutter masking the vocal, tasteful reverb on a send. Polished means controlled and intentional, not lifeless — leave some dynamics and breath.

## How do I mix rock — drum punch, guitar carving, glue

Rock lives on drum punch and guitars that carve their own space without turning to mush. For drum snap, compress the snare/kick with a slow attack (10-30 ms) and fast release (40-80 ms) so the transient cracks through; parallel-compress the drum bus for body. Guitars and vocals both want 1-4 kHz, so carve: dip the guitars a few dB where the vocal's presence sits (around 2-4 kHz) so the vocal cuts through, and high-pass the guitars around 80-120 Hz to keep the low end for bass and kick. Glue the whole kit (and even the mix) with light bus compression on an Aux Input — 2-4 dB at a low ratio (2:1), slow attack — so it feels like one band in one room. A little Saturator adds the harmonic grit and energy rock thrives on.

## How do I mix R&B and soul — warm, lush, spacious

R&B/soul is warm, intimate, and roomy — the opposite of a dry, in-your-face pop vocal. Keep the vocal smooth and lush: gentle compression (3:1, 3-6 dB), a touch of Saturator for warmth, and a small low-mid lift around 200-400 Hz for body rather than a harsh high boost. Give it space with reverb and delay on sends (make an Aux Input as a reverb return) — longer, lusher plate/hall tails than pop, but still tucked under the vocal so it's felt more than heard. Use the FX-Throw bar to sling tempo-synced echoes on the ends of phrases for that dreamy tail. Layer and pan backing vocal stacks wide to build a bed around the lead. Warmth comes from saturation and gentle EQ, not just turning up the bass.

## How do I get a lo-fi sound — saturation, noise, narrow

Lo-fi is about controlled imperfection: warmth, grit, narrowness, and softness instead of clarity. Drive a Saturator for analog warmth and harmonic dirt, then roll off the top with EQ-6 — a low-pass around 8-12 kHz instantly darkens and "tapes" the sound (drop the cutoff lower for a duller, more cassette feel). Add a high-pass (around 100-200 Hz) to thin out the lows and make it small and old-radio. Narrow the stereo image (keep things center or barely panned) for that mono, intimate feel. Add character with the Tape Delay for wobble and timing slop. Skip the Cleanup denoiser entirely here — for lo-fi you want hiss and hum, not removal — and if you have a vinyl-crackle/noise sample, drop it on its own track under the mix. Lean into pitch imperfection (don't reach for Tune) rather than correcting it out. The whole point is to break the hi-fi rules on purpose.

## What's the fastest way to get a genre-appropriate mix

Start with the auto-helpers, then steer by ear toward the genre's feel. Run Auto-mix to get balanced, bounded levels as a neutral starting point, then push the elements that matter for your style — vocal way up for pop/hip-hop, kick and sub forward for EDM/trap, drums and guitars driving for rock. Hit Vocal Doctor 🩺 on the lead for an instant pro vocal chain and nudge the 6 macro sliders (Bright/Warm/Smooth/De-Ess/Space/Throw) toward the vibe: more Bright + Throw for pop, more Warm + Space for R&B. Set loudness last with the Maximizer to the genre's ballpark. Reference a commercial track in the same genre at matched volume — your ears comparing A/B beats any preset. The auto tools get you 80% there fast; taste closes the last 20%.

## Are these genre rules or just conventions

They're conventions — common starting points that listeners expect, not laws. "Vocal upfront for pop," "loud master for EDM," "warm and roomy for R&B" describe what usually works for that audience, but every memorable record breaks some of them on purpose. A buried, lo-fi vocal can be more emotional than a polished one; a quiet, dynamic master can hit harder than a crushed one; mono can feel bigger than wide. Use the numbers in these notes as places to begin, then trust the song and your ears. If a "wrong" move sounds right and serves the emotion, it's right. Reference tracks tell you the convention; the music tells you when to ignore it.
