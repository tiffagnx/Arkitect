# Creative Delay Tricks

## The dotted-1/8 vocal delay — the classic rhythmic echo
The dotted-eighth delay is the most famous vocal delay sound (The Edge/U2, modern pop). A dotted 1/8 is 3/4 of a quarter note, so the repeats land on the off-beats and lock to the groove instead of fighting the lead vocal. Time it to tempo: dotted-1/8 ms = 45000 / BPM, so at 120 BPM it's 375 ms, at 140 BPM ~321 ms. In Studio put Tape Delay (or BPM Delay) on the vocal's send/aux, set the time to a dotted 1/8, feedback low (15-30% = 2-4 audible repeats), and mix it under the dry vocal. Roll the delay return darker — high-pass ~300 Hz, low-pass ~5-8 kHz on DeMartin EQ — so the echoes sit behind the words. Keep it on a send, not an insert, so the dry vocal stays fully present.

## How do I set delay time to the tempo / note values in ms
Tempo-synced delay just means the repeat time equals a musical note value at the song's BPM. The quarter-note (1/4) time in ms is 60000 / BPM. From there: 1/8 = half of that, 1/16 = a quarter of it, dotted-1/8 = 1.5x an 1/8, triplet-1/8 = 2/3 of a 1/8. At 120 BPM: 1/4 = 500 ms, 1/8 = 250 ms, dotted-1/8 = 375 ms, 1/16 = 125 ms. In Studio, Tape Delay and BPM Delay can sync to note divisions, and the Bars|Beats timebase shows the grid so you can eyeball whether repeats land on the beat. As a starting rule use 1/8 or dotted-1/8 for vocals, 1/4 for spacious leads, 1/16 for fast stutters. If repeats clutter the lyric, switch to a longer division or cut feedback.

## Ping-pong delay — echoes bouncing left to right
Ping-pong delay sends each repeat to the opposite side, so echoes bounce L-R-L-R and widen the stereo image. It's great on ad-libs, instrumental hooks, and anything you want to feel huge without piling up in the center. Studio doesn't have a one-click ping-pong mode yet, so build it: send to two delay auxes, one panned hard left and one hard right, set both to the same tempo-synced time but offset one by exactly half the repeat (e.g. left = 1/4, right = 1/4 but delayed an extra 1/8), and feed a little of each into the other for the bounce. Easier version: one delay aux panned with a short Haas offset between channels. Keep the dry vocal mono-centered so only the echoes move. High-pass the return ~250 Hz so the lows stay glued.

## Filtered lo-fi / "analog" delay — making echoes darker each repeat
Real analog (tape and bucket-brigade) delays lose top end and add saturation on every pass, so the echoes get darker and grittier as they fade — that's what makes them sit behind the dry track instead of cluttering it. Recreate it in Studio with Tape Delay (it already models tape-style wow and roll-off) or by EQ-ing a delay aux: low-pass the return around 3-6 kHz and high-pass around 200-400 Hz with DeMartin EQ, then add a touch of Saturator for warmth and grit. Feedback 25-40% gives a few decaying repeats. Push the low-pass lower for a more "underwater" vintage vibe. This is also how you keep a busy delay from masking consonants — a bright, full-range echo competes with the dry vocal; a dark one supports it.

## Ducked delay — echoes that only bloom in the gaps
A ducked delay stays quiet while the vocal is singing, then swells up in the pauses so the repeats fill the holes without smearing the lyric. It's the pro move that lets you run a big, lush delay and still keep the words crisp. The textbook way is a sidechain: the dry vocal ducks the delay return ~6-10 dB while the vocal is present, then it recovers in the gaps. Studio's Compressor doesn't expose a sidechain key input in the UI yet, so do it by hand: automate the delay send (or the aux fader) down during phrases and back up in the spaces — that's the same shape a sidechain would draw. A simpler trick that needs no automation: just run the delay darker and lower (EQ the return down, drop the send) so it never competes in the first place. Same idea works on reverb returns.

## Delay throw on the last word — the tempo-synced FX throw
A delay throw drenches one moment — usually the last word of a line — in tempo-synced echoes that bloom into the empty space before the next phrase, then fall away. It adds drama and motion without echoing the whole vocal. Easiest in Studio: use the FX-Throw bar, which does tempo-synced delay (and reverb) throws on a whole selection OR a single word — select just the last word and throw it. Manual way: automate the delay send up only for that word (sends banks A-J), or copy the word to a parallel "throw" track that's 100% wet delay. Time it to a musical division (1/4 or dotted-1/8) so the repeats stay in the pocket, and make sure the throw lands in a gap so the next line doesn't step on it. Throws live in the holes; if they collide, shorten the tail or cut feedback.

## Word-level vs whole-line FX throws — when to use each
A whole-selection throw treats a phrase or section as one chunk and echoes all of it; a word-level throw isolates a single word so only that syllable repeats. Word-level is the tasteful, modern choice for lead vocals — it spotlights one moment ("...goodbye-bye-bye") and keeps the rest of the line dry and intelligible. Whole-line throws fit ad-libs, instrumental phrases, breakdowns, and "drop the band out" effect moments. In Studio the FX-Throw bar supports both: make a selection across the whole line, or zoom in and select just the word. Both are tempo-synced, so the repeats lock to the BPM either way. Rule of thumb: throw the LAST word into a gap, leave the consonants that carry the lyric dry, and never throw two lines in a row or it stops being special.

## Self-oscillation and dub sirens — runaway feedback risers
Push a delay's feedback past ~100% and the repeats stop decaying and start building — that screaming, rising "dub siren" or laser sweep you hear in dub and EDM transitions. The repeats feed back into themselves and snowball, and sweeping the delay time while it oscillates bends the pitch up or down. In Studio, crank Tape Delay feedback toward maximum on a send (never on the dry insert) and ride the feedback and time controls by hand into a drop or transition. CAUTION: self-oscillation can get very loud fast — put a Maximizer/limiter or the master ceiling after it, start with the send low, and automate it back down before it overwhelms the mix. Filter the runaway with a low-pass so it screams rather than shreds. It's an effect for moments, not a whole song.

## Reverse delay — the echo that swells up before the word
Reverse delay plays the echoes backwards so they crescendo INTO the dry hit instead of trailing after it — a swooshy pre-echo that pulls the ear toward the next word. There's no one-click reverse-delay yet, so build it. Duplicate the word/phrase (Ctrl+D) to keep the dry original, then on the copy: Reverse the clip, put Tape Delay on its track and run Commit (print FX) on that track so the delayed repeats become real audio, then Reverse the clip again. Now the repeats play backwards, building up to where the dry word lands. Drag the swell so its peak hits exactly on the dry attack, and use an Equal-Power fade-in so it rises smoothly. Keep the dry word on top so the consonant still bites. Great on a verse opener or a chorus entry.

## Haas micro-delay for width — making a mono sound wide
The Haas effect uses a tiny delay (under ~30-40 ms) between left and right so your ear hears one wider source instead of an echo. It's the fastest way to widen a mono vocal double, a guitar, or a synth without an actual stereo recording. Starting point: duplicate the track, pan one copy hard left and one hard right, and nudge one copy later by 8-25 ms (slip it on the grid, or use a short slapback delay panned to one side). More delay = wider but more risk of a slap; under ~10 ms is subtle, 20-30 ms is dramatic. The big caution: Haas widening can collapse or sound hollow in mono, so always check the mono sum — if it thins out, reduce the offset or lower the side level. Keep the lead vocal centered and Haas the doubles, not the main.

## How is delay different from reverb — and when to reach for which
Delay gives you distinct, countable repeats; reverb gives a smeared wash of hundreds of reflections. Delay is rhythmic and clear, so it adds space and motion while keeping the vocal upfront — that's why modern pop leans on tempo-synced delay throws more than long reverbs. Reverb sets a room or mood but can muddy a busy mix if it's too long or too wet. They stack well: a short slapback or dotted-1/8 delay for rhythm, plus a touch of reverb on the delay's tail for glue. In Studio, both live best on sends/auxes so many tracks share them and you can EQ and automate the wet. Quick call: if you want the vocal to feel rhythmic and present, reach for delay; if you want it to feel placed in a space, reach for reverb; for drama on one word, throw the delay.

## Why does my delay make the vocal muddy or cluttered
A delay clutters the mix when the echoes are too loud, too bright, too many, or off the grid. Fixes, in order: lower the send so the repeats sit clearly under the dry vocal; cut feedback to 15-30% so you get 2-4 repeats instead of a wash; EQ the delay return darker — high-pass ~250-400 Hz and low-pass ~5-7 kHz on DeMartin EQ so echoes don't fight consonants; and sync the time to a note value so repeats land on the beat instead of smearing. If it still buries the lyric, duck it: automate the delay send (or aux fader) down during phrases and up in the gaps so the echoes only bloom in the holes. A De-Ess before the delay send stops sibilant esses from splashing into the repeats. Bright, loud, full-range delay masks words; dark, ducked, rhythmic delay supports them.
