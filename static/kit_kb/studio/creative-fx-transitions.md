# Creative FX & Transitions (the trick box)

## How do I make a stutter / glitch / beat-repeat effect
A stutter is a tiny slice of audio retriggered fast — the "d-d-d-drop" on a vocal or the machine-gun snare before a hit. Easiest way in Studio: select the spot, hit Chop to 1/16 to split it into sixteenth-note pieces, then keep one slice, delete the rest of the bar, and Duplicate it (Ctrl+D) back-to-back to repeat it 4-8 times. For an accelerating stutter, chop finer toward the end — paste 1/16s, then 1/32-spaced copies, so the repeats speed up into the downbeat. Trim every slice to a zero-cross and add a fast Equal-Power fade-in (2-5 ms) on each so they don't click. For a gated-rhythm chop, put a Gate on the track and let fast attack/release pulse holes in a sustained pad. Tighten by ear to the BPM in Bars|Beats timebase; commit when it grooves.

## How do I make a riser / uplifter / sweep before the drop
A riser is a sound that climbs in pitch, brightness, or volume to pull tension up into a section change. The classic build: take a noise loop, white-noise sample, or even a reversed cymbal, and over 2-8 bars open a low-pass filter from ~200 Hz up to ~15 kHz while the level rises. In Studio, automate DeMartin EQ — start with a steep low-pass low, sweep its cutoff up across the bars, and ride the track fader up at the same time. Layer a tonal riser (a synth note or vocal "ahh") pitched up over the bar using DeMartin Tune for a vari-speed climb. Add reverb/Tape Delay on a send that opens as it rises. A downlifter/downsweep is the mirror image — sweep the filter DOWN to deflate energy after the drop. Pro move: pair an uplifter going up with a downlifter at the exact transition so the swap feels physical.

## How do I make a whoosh / impact / boom hit
Whooshes smooth a transition; impacts mark the landing. A whoosh is a short noise sweep with movement — grab noise or a reversed crash, automate DeMartin EQ to sweep the cutoff and pan across ~1 bar, and drown it in reverb (decay 1.5-3 s) for the airy tail. An impact/boom is the thud on the downbeat of a new section: a low sine/kick layer (40-80 Hz) plus a bright transient (a reversed-then-forward crash or a clap) stacked on beat 1. Build it in Studio by layering two clips on a track, low-passing the boom layer and high-passing the transient so they don't fight, then Saturator for weight. A reverse-cymbal swell INTO the impact (Reverse a crash, line its peak to the downbeat) is the most reliable "here it comes" cue. Keep the boom's low end clean by high-passing any reverb or layers under ~40 Hz and short-fading the boom's tail so it doesn't smear into the next kick. (Studio has no sidechain key on the Compressor — that's the right tool for ducking the boom under the kick, so it's a gap.)

## How do I build the drop — tension, silence, then the hit
The drop is sold by what happens BEFORE it, not the hit itself. Standard recipe: 1) in the bar or two before, automate a low-pass filter (DeMartin EQ) to slowly close so the track gets darker and the energy "ducks under water." 2) Add a snare/clap roll that doubles in speed into the downbeat — Chop to 1/16 then duplicate finer (1/16 → 1/32) so it accelerates. 3) Stack a riser/uplifter sweeping up over the same span. 4) Cut a moment of near-silence right before the hit — drop the full mix to just the riser tail or to dead air for a beat (an Insert Silence or a hard fade). 5) Land the drop with an impact on beat 1 (boom + bright transient) and bring the full arrangement back wide and loud. The silence is the secret — your ear braces for the void and the hit feels huge. Length of the gap is taste: a half-beat is subtle, a full beat is dramatic.

## How do I make a tape-stop or vinyl-stop effect
A tape-stop slows the audio down to a dead halt with a falling pitch — the "power off" effect at the end of a phrase or before a drop. Studio has no one-click tape-stop, so build it from clip ops: take the clip, and over ~0.3-0.8 s ramp its playback speed/pitch down to zero. The practical workaround is to Duplicate the clip, use DeMartin Tune / vari-speed to pitch the tail down progressively (e.g. step it -2, -5, -9, -14 semitones across short slices), and add a fast fade-out so it settles into silence. A vinyl-stop is the same gesture but shorter and with a faster drop plus a touch of low-end "wow." Add a quick Tape Delay sputter and some Saturator dirt for the analog feel. Print it once it sounds right. This is the #1 transition-FX gap in the DAW — flag it.

## How do I make a telephone / radio / megaphone vocal
These "small box" effects all come from aggressive band-pass EQ plus distortion. For a telephone/lo-fi vocal: on DeMartin EQ or EQ-6, high-pass hard around 300-400 Hz and low-pass around 3-3.5 kHz so only the midrange survives — that's the phone band. Add a little Saturator for the crunchy, compressed phone-line grit, and a touch of Compressor to flatten it. For a radio/CB voice push the distortion harder and narrow the band slightly more (500 Hz to 3 kHz). A megaphone/bullhorn is the same band-pass but with heavier saturation, a midrange honk (boost ~1-2 kHz a few dB), and a short slapback delay for the outdoor-PA bark. Use it on a duplicate of the vocal blended under the clean one, or automate it in only for one line so it pops as an effect. Adjust the band edges by ear — wider = more natural, narrower = more "device."

## How do I pitch a vocal down to a demon or up to a chipmunk
Pitch FX live in DeMartin Tune. For a "demon" voice, pitch the vocal DOWN an octave (-12 semitones) — keep formants natural for a deep, monstrous tone, or shift formants down too for a fuller beast. Layer the -12 octave UNDER the dry vocal at low level for a subtle thickening/menace, or solo it for full demon. For a chipmunk, pitch UP +12 (or +7 for less cartoonish); shifting formants up too sells the small-creature sound. The key knob is formant shift: move pitch and formant TOGETHER for the classic sped-up/slowed-down vari-speed sound, or move PITCH ONLY (formant held) to change note without the chipmunk/demon coloration — that's how you tune a vocal invisibly vs. as an effect. Start at whole-octave moves, then taste-tune the formant amount. Duplicate (Ctrl+D) first so you keep the dry take.

## How do I do screwed & chopped or half-time
Screwed & chopped is the Houston / DJ Screw sound: slow the whole track down (lower pitch and tempo together) and add stutter chops and skips. In Studio, drop the song tempo and use DeMartin Tune / vari-speed to pull pitch down with it (-2 to -5 semitones is a typical "screw"; deeper = sludgier). Then "chop" — Chop to 1/16, duplicate select slices to stutter words, and rearrange phrases. Add Tape Delay and a touch of reverb for the syrupy haze. Half-time is different: it makes the beat FEEL half as fast without changing the song's BPM — you keep the tempo but play the backbeat half as often, so instead of a snare on beats 2 AND 4 you hit it once per bar (on beat 3) and let the kick breathe, so a 140 BPM track feels like 70. Easiest in Studio: edit the drum pattern to half-time placement, or pitch/stretch a drum loop to double length. Half-time hits hardest as a contrast section after a busy part.

## How do I make a transition smoother between two sections
Transitions are about removing the seam and signaling the change. The cheapest, most reliable move is a crossfade: select across the boundary and use the Batch Fades dialog with an Equal-Power crossfade so energy stays constant through the overlap (Standard/linear can dip in the middle and sound like a hole). Layer a whoosh sweeping across the boundary and an impact on the first downbeat of the new section to mask the cut. A short filter dip — close a low-pass for the last beat of the old section and open it on the new — makes the swap feel deliberate. A reverb throw on the last word/note into the gap (FX-Throw bar) carries the ear across. For a hard cut, drop a beat of silence first so the new section punches. Match the transition's drama to the size of the change — verse-to-verse wants subtle, build-to-drop wants everything.

## What's the easy way vs the deep way to do these FX
Easy way: most trick-box moves have a fast version using one or two clip ops. Stutter = Chop to 1/16 + Duplicate. Riser = automate DeMartin EQ low-pass cutoff up. Telephone = band-pass EQ + Saturator. Pitch FX = DeMartin Tune octave up/down. Transition = Equal-Power crossfade + a whoosh + an impact. Reverb/delay throw = the FX-Throw bar on a word. Deep way: print and re-process for layered results — stack a tonal riser under a noise riser, build a multi-layer impact (sub boom + bright transient + reverse swell), or combine vari-speed pitch + chops + tape delay for a full screwed treatment. Always Duplicate (Ctrl+D) the dry source first and keep it muted nearby — these effects are destructive once committed. Commit (Print VERB / Bounce to Disk) only when the effect is final, so it locks in and frees CPU. None of this is law; the right amount is whatever serves the song.
